26  Common Pitfalls and How to Avoid Them

Estimated reading time: 50 minutes

A practical guide for avoiding frequent mistakes in causal inference and targeted learning

Even with a solid understanding of the Causal Roadmap and modern estimators, it is easy to make analytic choices that lead to biased, unstable, or misleading results. In real-world evidence (RWE), the stakes are higher: data are messy, treatment pathways are irregular, and credibility depends on careful alignment between the scientific question, identification conditions, and the estimator.

This chapter catalogs common pitfalls encountered in causal inference, especially in pharmacoepidemiologic and longitudinal RWE settings, and provides practical strategies to avoid them. Each pitfall is organized around a common mistake, an explanation of why it matters, and concrete guidance for avoidance. Where possible, we connect the pitfall to the target trial emulation framework and the estimand, since most errors can be traced to a breakdown in the chain from question to design to identification to estimation.

26.1 Pitfall: Confusing Association With Causation

26.1.1 The regression coefficient problem

Mistake: Fitting a multivariable regression and interpreting the treatment coefficient as a causal effect.

Why it matters: Regression coefficients are typically conditional associations, not causal contrasts. Unless the model corresponds to the identified statistical functional and is correctly specified, the result may not estimate the causal estimand. In pharmacoepidemiology, this often manifests when an analyst fits a Cox model with treatment as a covariate alongside confounders and interprets the hazard ratio as the causal effect of treatment. The coefficient reflects neither the marginal causal effect nor any well-defined intervention, particularly when the model includes variables on the causal pathway or when the hazard ratio is non-collapsible.

RWE example. A study of statin initiation and cardiovascular events fits a multivariable Cox model adjusting for age, sex, LDL cholesterol, diabetes, and smoking status. The analyst reports the adjusted hazard ratio as the “effect of statins.” However, if LDL is a mediator (statins lower LDL, which reduces cardiovascular risk), the coefficient does not correspond to the total causal effect. If the model is misspecified (e.g., the true relationship between age and the log-hazard is nonlinear), the coefficient is biased even for the conditional association.

Avoid by: - Defining a precise causal question and estimand before fitting any model. Use the estimand specification template above. - Estimating the corresponding statistical functional using g-computation, IPTW, AIPW, or TMLE, which explicitly separate the causal target from the nuisance estimation strategy. - Using flexible nuisance estimation (for example, SuperLearner) where appropriate, so that the result does not depend on a single parametric specification. - Drawing a DAG to determine which variables are confounders (must adjust), mediators (must not adjust for total effect), and colliders (must not condition on).

Diagnostics and sensitivity analysis. Compare the regression-based estimate with a properly identified estimate (e.g., TMLE with the same covariate set). If the two diverge substantially, investigate whether model misspecification, non-collapsibility, or mediator adjustment explains the difference. As a robustness check, vary the functional form assumptions in the regression (e.g., add splines for continuous confounders) and assess stability.

26.2 Pitfall: Adjusting for Post-Treatment Variables

26.2.1 The mediator/collider trap

Mistake: Including variables measured after treatment initiation in outcome regression or propensity score models.

Why it matters: - Post-treatment variables may lie on the causal pathway (mediators), so adjustment can block part of the effect - They may be colliders, inducing spurious associations - Adjustment can invalidate the intervention being emulated

This pitfall is directly connected to the target trial protocol. In a well-specified target trial, the adjustment set consists of baseline covariates measured at or before time zero. Any variable measured after time zero is, by definition, post-treatment and should not appear in the propensity score or baseline outcome model unless the analysis explicitly targets a mediation estimand or uses longitudinal methods designed for time-varying confounding.

RWE example. A study of biologic therapy for rheumatoid arthritis adjusts for C-reactive protein (CRP) measured three months after treatment initiation. CRP is affected by treatment (biologics reduce inflammation) and also predicts joint destruction (the outcome). Adjusting for three-month CRP blocks the indirect pathway through inflammation reduction, attenuating the estimated total effect. If the analyst instead wants the direct effect not mediated by CRP, that is a different estimand requiring a different identification strategy (e.g., natural direct effect estimation via mediation analysis).

Decision rule for time-varying confounding. If a covariate is (a) affected by prior treatment and (b) also a confounder of subsequent treatment and the outcome, then baseline-only adjustment is insufficient. This is the hallmark of time-varying confounding affected by prior treatment, and it requires longitudinal methods: IPTW for marginal structural models, longitudinal g-computation, or LTMLE. These methods adjust for the confounding without blocking the causal pathway, by modeling the treatment and censoring mechanisms at each time point rather than conditioning on intermediate values.

Avoid by: - Restricting adjustment to baseline confounders for baseline treatment effects, as specified in the target trial protocol. - Using longitudinal methods (for example, LTMLE or LMTP) when time-varying confounding affected by prior treatment is present. LTMLE targets the G-formula under sequential exchangeability, estimating the counterfactual outcome under sustained treatment strategies while respecting the temporal ordering of confounders, treatments, and outcomes. - Drawing DAGs to clarify temporal ordering and avoid collider bias. - When estimating a per-protocol effect (the effect of sustained adherence), treating adherence as part of the treatment strategy and using IPCW or LTMLE to adjust for post-baseline confounders of adherence, rather than simply conditioning on adherence status.

Diagnostics and sensitivity analysis. Examine whether any covariates in the adjustment set were measured after time zero. If time-varying confounders are present, compare estimates from baseline-only adjustment with those from IPTW or LTMLE to quantify the magnitude of time-varying confounding bias. As a sensitivity analysis, assess how results change when suspected mediators are removed from or added to the adjustment set.

26.3 Pitfall: Violated Positivity or Limited Overlap

26.3.1 Treatment not comparable across covariate strata

Symptoms: - Propensity scores close to 0 or 1 - Large or highly variable IPTW weights - Extreme clever covariate values in TMLE

Why it matters: Positivity violations can lead to instability, high variance, and in finite samples, estimand drift toward extrapolation. But the consequences extend beyond estimator performance. Positivity is an identifiability condition: if certain covariate strata contain patients who would never receive one of the treatment arms, the causal effect is not nonparametrically identified in those strata. No amount of modeling flexibility can recover information that the data do not contain. This is “practical positivity”; a statement about the data-generating process, not merely about the estimator.

RWE example. A study compares warfarin to direct oral anticoagulants (DOACs) for stroke prevention in atrial fibrillation. Patients aged over 90 with severe renal impairment almost never receive DOACs in the data (probability near zero). The ATE for this subgroup is not identifiable from the observed data. Including these patients inflates weights, destabilizes variance, and shifts the estimate toward extrapolation. The analyst must decide whether to restrict the population, change the estimand, or report the limitation.

26.3.2 Structured overlap diagnostic workflow

Positivity assessment should follow a structured sequence, not a single plot.

  1. Propensity score overlap plot. Plot the distribution of estimated propensity scores by treatment group (e.g., mirrored histograms or density plots). Look for regions where one group has near-zero density.

  2. Weight distribution. Compute stabilized IPTW weights and examine the distribution: maximum weight, ratio of maximum to median, and the proportion of total weight carried by the top 1% of observations. If a small number of observations carry a large share of the total weight, the estimate is fragile.

  3. Effective sample size (ESS). Calculate \(\text{ESS} = (\sum w_i)^2 / \sum w_i^2\) for each treatment group. If the ESS is substantially smaller than the actual sample size (e.g., less than half), the analysis is relying heavily on a small number of reweighted observations.

  4. Sensitivity to truncation. Estimate the treatment effect at several truncation thresholds (e.g., truncating weights at the 95th, 99th, and 99.5th percentiles). If the point estimate shifts substantially with truncation, the result depends on the tails of the weight distribution; a sign that the estimand may not be well-supported.

26.3.3 Decision rule for positivity failures

When diagnostics reveal positivity problems, the analyst faces three options, in order of preference:

  1. Restrict the population to common support. Exclude covariate strata where one treatment arm has near-zero probability. This changes the target population (and therefore the estimand) to one that is identifiable. Report the restriction transparently.

  2. Change the estimand to a support-aware alternative. When static interventions (“set treatment to 1 for everyone”) violate positivity, consider estimands defined by interventions that respect the natural treatment assignment:

    • Stochastic interventions define a policy that shifts the probability of treatment rather than setting it deterministically. For example, “increase the probability of DOAC initiation by 20% for each patient.” These estimands are identified even when some patients have near-zero probability of one treatment, because the intervention does not force treatment where it would not naturally occur.
    • Incremental propensity score interventions multiply the odds of treatment by a fixed factor (e.g., doubling the odds of treatment for everyone). These provide a smooth, interpretable contrast that avoids extrapolation.
    • Modified treatment policies (MTPs) define the intervention as a function of the natural value of treatment. For continuous or time-varying exposures, an MTP might specify “reduce dose by 10% at each time point relative to the dose the patient would naturally receive.” The lmtp package implements longitudinal MTPs with TMLE-based estimation.

    Redefining the estimand is not a concession of failure. It is often the correct scientific response to a structural feature of the data-generating process. If the data cannot support the original question, asking a closely related question that the data can answer is more informative than forcing an unstable answer to the original one.

  3. Report that the original estimand is not credibly identifiable. If neither restriction nor estimand modification produces a scientifically meaningful question, state explicitly that the data do not support the intended causal comparison. This is a legitimate and important finding.

Avoid by: - Inspecting propensity score overlap and weight distributions using the workflow above. - Truncating or stabilizing weights when justified, and reporting sensitivity to truncation level. - Restricting to regions of common support when positivity violations are concentrated in identifiable subgroups. - Considering stochastic interventions or modified treatment policies when static interventions are not feasible in the observed data.

Diagnostics and sensitivity analysis. Report the propensity score overlap plot, weight distribution summary statistics (max, ESS, proportion of weight in top 1%), and estimates under at least two truncation thresholds. If restricting the population, report the characteristics of excluded patients and discuss how the restriction changes the target population. Conduct a sensitivity analysis comparing the ATE on the full population with the ATE on the restricted population and, if feasible, with a stochastic intervention estimand.

26.4 Pitfall: Misspecified Outcome or Propensity Models

26.4.1 Relying on simple models in complex settings

Mistake: Assuming linearity, additivity, or simple parametric relationships without support.

Why it matters: Misspecification of nuisance functions can create bias, especially for non-collapsible estimands or when overlap is limited. For IPTW, misspecification of the propensity score model leads to biased weights and biased treatment effect estimates. For g-computation, misspecification of the outcome model directly biases the estimate. TMLE and AIPW offer double robustness; consistency if either the outcome model or the treatment model is correctly specified; but this protection is only asymptotic and depends on regularity conditions. In finite samples with substantial misspecification of both models, even doubly robust estimators can be biased.

RWE example. A study of metformin initiation and HbA1c reduction fits a linear outcome model adjusting for age, BMI, baseline HbA1c, and eGFR as main effects. However, the relationship between baseline HbA1c and the outcome is strongly nonlinear (patients with higher baseline HbA1c have more room for reduction), and there is an interaction between BMI and metformin effect. The linear model misses both features. When the outcome model is used in g-computation, the estimated ATE is biased. When used as the initial estimate in TMLE, the targeting step can partially correct the bias if the propensity score model is well-specified, but the correction is incomplete if the propensity score model is also too rigid.

Avoid by: - Using SuperLearner or other data-adaptive estimation with cross-validation to reduce dependence on a single parametric specification. SuperLearner combines multiple candidate learners (e.g., generalized linear models, random forests, gradient boosting, multivariate adaptive regression splines) using cross-validated risk to select the optimal weighted combination. - Inspecting calibration and predictive performance (for example, cross-validated risk, calibration plots for propensity scores). - Including domain-informed learners alongside flexible algorithms when strong structure is known (e.g., a parametric learner that includes a known interaction, alongside data-adaptive learners that can discover additional structure).

Diagnostics and sensitivity analysis. Report the cross-validated risk of the SuperLearner ensemble and the relative contribution of each candidate learner. For propensity scores, produce calibration plots (predicted probability versus observed treatment proportion in deciles). Compare estimates from a simple parametric model with estimates from SuperLearner-based TMLE. If the two diverge, investigate which model features drive the difference. As a robustness check, vary the SuperLearner library (e.g., add or remove highly flexible learners) and assess stability of the treatment effect estimate.

26.5 Pitfall: Blindly Trusting Machine Learning

26.5.1 Flexible models do not guarantee valid inference

Mistake: Assuming that a strong predictive model implies a correct causal estimate.

Why it matters: Machine learning can overfit, extrapolate, or yield poorly calibrated probabilities. Prediction quality alone does not ensure correct estimation of a causal functional. A model that achieves excellent cross-validated AUC for treatment prediction is not necessarily a good propensity score model for causal inference: the model may be well-calibrated on average but poorly calibrated in the tails, precisely where causal estimation is most sensitive. Furthermore, a highly flexible learner fit on the full sample can produce plug-in bias; the nuisance parameter estimate overfits to the sample in a way that biases the final causal estimate, even asymptotically, unless proper sample-splitting is used.

26.5.2 Prediction versus causal estimation

The objective in nuisance estimation is not to maximize prediction accuracy in the population. It is to obtain consistent estimates of nuisance functions (the outcome regression and the treatment/censoring mechanisms) that, when plugged into the causal functional, yield a consistent and efficient estimate of the target parameter. A model that is overfit to the training data will produce nuisance estimates that are too responsive to sample-specific noise. When these are plugged into the TMLE or AIPW estimator, the result inherits that noise, producing standard errors that are too narrow and confidence intervals with incorrect coverage.

26.5.3 Cross-fitting as a guardrail

Cross-fitting (also called sample splitting) partitions the data into K folds. For each fold, nuisance functions are estimated on the remaining K-1 folds and evaluated on the held-out fold. The causal parameter is then estimated using the cross-fitted nuisance predictions. This eliminates the Donsker-class condition that would otherwise be required for consistency when using highly adaptive learners (e.g., random forests, boosted trees, neural networks). Cross-validated TMLE (CV-TMLE) implements this approach and should be preferred whenever the SuperLearner library includes learners that are more complex than parametric regression.

26.5.4 ML leakage checklist

Before fitting any machine learning model for nuisance estimation, verify:

  • No future information. Features used to predict treatment at time \(t\) must be measured at or before time \(t\). Features used to predict the outcome must not include post-treatment variables unless the estimand explicitly requires them (e.g., mediation).
  • No post-baseline variables in the propensity score model unless the analysis uses a longitudinal framework (LTMLE) that explicitly models the time-varying treatment mechanism.
  • No biased feature construction. Derived variables (e.g., “number of prior hospitalizations”) must not inadvertently encode outcome information or use look-ahead data.
  • No outcome information in the treatment model. The propensity score model must not include the outcome or variables determined after the outcome.

26.5.5 Targeted learning diagnostics

When using TMLE or AIPW, the following diagnostics help assess whether nuisance estimation is adequate for causal inference:

Clever covariate extremes. In TMLE, the clever covariate \(H(A, W) = I(A=1)/g(W) - I(A=0)/(1-g(W))\) drives the targeting step. When propensity scores \(g(W)\) are near 0 or 1, the clever covariate becomes extreme, and the targeting step can produce large, unstable updates. Inspect the distribution of clever covariate values. If a small number of observations have values orders of magnitude larger than the rest, those observations dominate the targeting step and may destabilize the estimate.

Influence curve stability. The efficient influence curve (EIC) provides observation-level contributions to the parameter estimate. Compute the EIC for each observation and inspect the distribution. Observations with EIC values far from the mean (e.g., beyond 5 standard deviations) exert disproportionate influence. Investigate whether these correspond to positivity violations, extreme covariate values, or data errors.

Collaborative targeting. When nuisance estimation is challenging (e.g., rare outcomes, near-violations of positivity), standard TMLE may benefit from collaborative targeted learning approaches that jointly optimize the outcome and treatment models to minimize the variance of the target parameter, rather than optimizing each model independently. This is conceptually similar to choosing the propensity score model that produces the most efficient TMLE, rather than the one that best predicts treatment. When standard diagnostics reveal instability, consider whether collaborative targeting or regularization of the targeting step improves performance.

Avoid by: - Using strict cross-validation and cross-fitting (CV-TMLE) when the learner library includes flexible algorithms. - Checking calibration (especially for propensity scores) and inspecting clever covariate distributions. - Using TMLE or other doubly robust approaches to reduce sensitivity to nuisance estimation errors, subject to regularity conditions. - Applying the ML leakage checklist before model fitting.

Diagnostics and sensitivity analysis. Report cross-validated risk for each nuisance model, propensity score calibration plots, the distribution of clever covariate values, and a summary of influence curve extremes (e.g., number of observations with EIC values beyond \(\pm 3\) or \(\pm 5\) standard deviations of the mean). As a sensitivity analysis, compare estimates from CV-TMLE with estimates from non-cross-fitted TMLE. If they differ substantially, overfitting of nuisance models is likely a concern.

26.6 Pitfall: Ignoring Censoring and Informative Dropout

Mistake: Treating censoring as non-informative without justification, or ignoring treatment discontinuation and switching when they affect the estimand.

Why it matters: Informative censoring can bias estimates of risk and survival functionals. In pharmacoepidemiology, censoring is rarely truly random. Patients who discontinue treatment, switch to a competitor drug, transfer care, or die from competing causes are typically different from those who remain under observation. If the factors driving censoring also affect the outcome (informative censoring), naive analyses that treat censored observations as non-informative will be biased.

The connection to the estimand is critical. Whether treatment discontinuation or switching constitutes “censoring” or is part of the estimand depends on the causal question. Under a treatment-policy (intention-to-treat analog) estimand, switching is part of the natural course and patients are followed regardless. Under a per-protocol estimand, deviation from the assigned strategy is an intercurrent event that must be handled; either through censoring at deviation and IPCW adjustment, or through a hypothetical estimand that models the counterfactual under sustained adherence.

RWE example. A study compares two antihypertensive regimens for a composite cardiovascular endpoint over five years. Twenty percent of patients in one arm switch to the other within two years, and switching is more common among patients with poorly controlled blood pressure (a predictor of the outcome). Under a per-protocol estimand, naive censoring at switch removes the highest-risk patients from one arm, biasing the estimate toward the null. Under a treatment-policy estimand, all patients are followed regardless of switching, but the estimate reflects the real-world treatment trajectory (including switching) rather than the pharmacological effect of sustained therapy.

Avoid by: - Defining censoring relative to the estimand in the target trial protocol. Distinguish administrative censoring (end of data availability, unrelated to health status) from informative censoring (loss to follow-up, switching, competing events). - Modeling censoring mechanisms (for example, with SuperLearner) and applying IPCW when the censoring mechanism depends on measured covariates. - Using TMLE/LTMLE with explicit censoring nodes in longitudinal settings. LTMLE jointly models the treatment, censoring, and outcome processes at each time point, providing doubly robust estimation under sequential exchangeability. - Specifying how adherence and switching are handled in the estimand. Switching can be part of the treatment strategy (treatment-policy), a censoring event (per-protocol with IPCW), or the basis of a hypothetical estimand (what would have happened if no one switched). The choice determines the analysis method.

Diagnostics and sensitivity analysis. Report the rate and timing of censoring by treatment arm and by key baseline covariates. If IPCW is used, report the distribution of censoring weights and the effective sample size. Compare estimates from (a) naive complete-case analysis, (b) IPCW-adjusted analysis, and (c) LTMLE with censoring nodes. As a sensitivity analysis, assess how the estimate changes under plausible violations of the independent censoring assumption: for example, assume that censored patients have 20%, 50%, or 100% higher event rates than non-censored patients with the same covariates, and bound the resulting bias.

26.7 Pitfall: Over-interpreting Heterogeneous Treatment Effects

26.7.1 Subgroup and HTE analyses are fragile

Mistake: Treating exploratory subgroup findings as confirmatory evidence.

Why it matters: HTE estimates are often high variance, subject to multiple testing, and sensitive to modeling choices. In pharmacoepidemiology, it is common to examine treatment effects within subgroups defined by age, sex, comorbidity, or biomarker status. The combination of many potential subgroups with small subgroup-specific sample sizes creates a high risk of spurious findings. A treatment that appears beneficial in patients with CKD but harmful in patients without CKD may simply reflect statistical noise, particularly if the subgroup analysis was not pre-specified.

The problem is compounded when data-adaptive methods are used to discover subgroups. Causal forests, for example, can identify regions of the covariate space with heterogeneous treatment effects, but the resulting estimates are inherently exploratory. The same flexibility that allows the algorithm to detect heterogeneity also allows it to find patterns that do not replicate. Without external validation or a confirmatory design, subgroup-specific treatment effects discovered by machine learning should be treated as hypotheses, not conclusions.

RWE example. A study of a new anticoagulant reports that the drug reduces stroke in patients over 75 but increases bleeding in patients under 65. The subgroup analysis was conducted post hoc across 12 subgroups defined by age, sex, renal function, and concomitant medications. After accounting for multiple comparisons, neither finding reaches conventional significance. The headline finding of differential effects by age is likely an artifact of multiple testing.

Avoid by: - Pre-specifying subgroups and hypotheses in the analysis plan, ideally motivated by biological plausibility or prior evidence. - Reporting uncertainty and accounting for multiplicity where relevant. At minimum, report confidence intervals for subgroup-specific effects and note the number of subgroups examined. - Treating causal forest or related approaches as exploratory unless paired with confirmatory design (e.g., prospective validation in independent data). - Using targeted-learning-based approaches (for example, TMLE-based variable importance) when aligned with the scientific objective. These approaches can provide valid inference for pre-specified effect modification questions while using flexible modeling for nuisance estimation.

Diagnostics and sensitivity analysis. Report the number of subgroups examined and whether they were pre-specified. For data-adaptive HTE methods, report cross-validated estimates and honest confidence intervals (e.g., from honest causal forests). As a sensitivity analysis, assess whether the identified heterogeneity pattern is robust to changes in the learner library, the covariate set, or the definition of subgroups. If a finding is not robust to reasonable analytic variations, it should be presented as preliminary.

26.8 Pitfall: Using the Wrong Estimand

26.8.1 Odds ratios and hazard ratios are commonly misinterpreted

Mistake: Defaulting to odds ratios or hazard ratios and interpreting them as causal effects without careful justification.

Why it matters: - Odds ratios are non-collapsible and may not correspond to the causal contrast of interest. A conditional odds ratio from a logistic regression generally differs from the marginal odds ratio even when there is no confounding, because the odds ratio does not average linearly across strata. - Hazard ratios are often difficult to interpret causally in the presence of time-varying hazards and selection into the risk set. Even in a randomized trial, the hazard ratio at time \(t\) conditions on survival to time \(t\), which is affected by prior treatment. If treatment affects early survival, the risk sets at later times are no longer comparable between arms, and the hazard ratio reflects both the treatment effect and the selection of survivors. This built-in selection bias means that hazard ratios do not have a clean causal interpretation in most settings.

26.8.2 Why hazard ratios are problematic as causal estimands

The Cox proportional hazards model estimates a hazard ratio under the assumption that the ratio of hazard functions is constant over time. When this assumption holds, the hazard ratio provides a compact summary. However:

  1. Non-collapsibility. The conditional hazard ratio (adjusted for covariates) generally differs from the marginal hazard ratio, even when the covariates are not confounders. This is an intrinsic property of the hazard ratio as a summary measure, not a sign of confounding.
  2. Built-in selection bias. At any time \(t\), the hazard ratio compares event rates among patients who have survived to time \(t\). If treatment affects survival before time \(t\), the surviving populations in the two arms differ in ways that may not be fully captured by measured covariates. The hazard ratio at time \(t\) therefore reflects both the treatment effect and this differential survival selection.
  3. Non-proportionality. When hazards are non-proportional (as is common in pharmacoepidemiology, e.g., early benefit that wanes, or delayed treatment effects), a single hazard ratio is misleading as a summary.

26.8.3 Preferred alternatives for survival outcomes

  • Survival probability at clinically meaningful time points (e.g., 1-year survival, 5-year survival). Directly interpretable and can be estimated with g-computation, IPTW, or TMLE.
  • Risk difference or risk ratio at a fixed time horizon (e.g., 90-day risk difference). Provides an absolute or relative measure on a scale that clinicians and patients can act on.
  • Restricted mean survival time (RMST) to a specified time horizon \(\tau\). The RMST difference gives the expected gain or loss in event-free survival time over the interval \([0, \tau]\). It does not require proportional hazards and is interpretable even when hazard ratios are not.
  • Cumulative incidence under competing risks. When a competing event (e.g., death from non-cardiac causes) can preclude the outcome of interest (e.g., cardiac hospitalization), the cause-specific hazard approach conditions on surviving the competing event, which may not reflect a scientifically meaningful quantity. The cumulative incidence function (or sub-distribution hazard via Fine-Gray) accounts for the competing event, but care is needed: the estimand is the probability of the event in the real world where the competing event also occurs, not the probability in a world without competing events.

26.8.4 Competing risks decision rule

When a competing event is present, determine whether it:

  1. Changes the meaning of the estimand. If the competing event (e.g., death) precludes the outcome, the cause-specific hazard estimates the rate of the outcome among those still alive; a quantity that conditions on an intermediate event affected by treatment. This is often not the estimand of interest.
  2. Should be incorporated into the estimand. Options include the cumulative incidence (probability of the event by time \(t\) in the presence of the competing risk), a composite endpoint (event or competing event, whichever comes first), or a treatment-policy approach that follows all patients regardless of competing events (using, e.g., the separable effects framework or total effect on a composite).
  3. Requires its own sensitivity analysis. Vary the handling of the competing event (e.g., compare cause-specific analysis with competing-risk analysis) and assess how conclusions change.

Avoid by: - Targeting risk differences, risk ratios, survival curves, cumulative incidence, or RMST when scientifically appropriate. State the estimand explicitly using the estimand specification template. - Using methods designed for causal survival analysis (including targeted learning approaches, the concrete package for competing risks, or LTMLE for time-to-event outcomes) when time-to-event outcomes are primary. - Reporting hazard ratios only as supplementary measures, with explicit caveats about non-collapsibility and selection bias, and always alongside absolute risk measures.

Diagnostics and sensitivity analysis. Test the proportional hazards assumption (e.g., Schoenfeld residuals, log-log survival plots). If non-proportionality is detected, report time-varying hazard ratios or, preferably, switch to RMST or survival probability contrasts. When competing risks are present, compare cause-specific and competing-risk analyses and report how the estimate changes. As a sensitivity analysis, vary the time horizon for risk difference or RMST estimation to assess whether conclusions are robust to the choice of follow-up window.

26.9 Pitfall: Not Performing Diagnostics

26.9.1 “The model ran” does not imply validity

Mistake: Reporting results without checking whether key assumptions are approximately supported in the observed data.

Why it matters: Many failures in applied causal inference arise from detectable issues such as overlap problems, extreme weights, or unstable influence curves. The danger is not that these problems are invisible; it is that analysts often skip the diagnostics that would reveal them. In targeted learning, the EIC provides a built-in diagnostic: if the estimated EIC has extreme values, the estimate is driven by a small number of observations, and the standard error may understate the true uncertainty. In IPTW, the weight distribution directly reveals whether the analysis is well-supported by the data or is extrapolating from a handful of reweighted observations.

RWE example. A TMLE analysis of a new diabetes medication reports a risk difference of -0.04 (95% CI: -0.06 to -0.02), suggesting a 4-percentage-point reduction in cardiovascular events. However, inspection of the influence curve reveals that three observations (out of 50,000) have EIC values that are 50 times the standard deviation of the remaining observations. These three patients have propensity scores below 0.01, and the clever covariate drives the targeting step to assign them enormous influence. The reported confidence interval does not account for this instability. After removing these observations (or equivalently, truncating the propensity score at 0.01), the estimate shifts to -0.02 with a wider confidence interval. The original result was not robust.

26.9.2 A diagnostic checklist for causal analyses

  1. Propensity score overlap. Plot distributions by treatment group. Flag regions with near-zero density.
  2. Weight distributions. Report max, mean, median, ESS, and the proportion of total weight in the top 1% of observations.
  3. Clever covariate distribution (TMLE-specific). Report the range and identify extreme values. Values orders of magnitude larger than the median indicate near-positivity violations.
  4. Influence curve distribution. Compute the EIC for each observation. Report the standard deviation and the number of observations beyond \(\pm 3\) and \(\pm 5\) SDs. Investigate outliers.
  5. Nuisance model calibration. For the propensity score, produce calibration plots (decile-based observed vs. predicted treatment probability). For the outcome model, report cross-validated risk and, if possible, calibration in relevant subgroups.
  6. Covariate balance after weighting. Compute standardized mean differences (SMDs) for all covariates, before and after weighting. Residual imbalance (SMD > 0.1) after weighting suggests misspecification of the propensity score model.

Avoid by: - Inspecting weight distributions and effective sample sizes. - Checking clever covariate ranges and outliers in targeted learning. - Examining influence curve stability and identifying influential observations. - Evaluating nuisance model calibration and cross-validated risk. - Treating diagnostics as a required component of the analysis, not an optional supplement.

Diagnostics and sensitivity analysis. Report all items from the diagnostic checklist above. As a sensitivity analysis, re-estimate the treatment effect after removing the most influential observations (e.g., top 0.1% by absolute EIC value) and assess stability. If the estimate changes substantially, the result depends on a small number of observations and should be interpreted with caution.

26.10 Pitfall: Neglecting Sensitivity Analyses

26.10.1 Unmeasured confounding is not solved by optimism

Mistake: Assuming exchangeability holds without probing robustness.

Why it matters: In observational settings, residual confounding is frequently plausible and may change qualitative conclusions. No design or analytic approach can eliminate unmeasured confounding from observational data. The best an analyst can do is (a) make the no unmeasured confounding assumption as plausible as possible through careful design and covariate selection, and (b) assess how sensitive the conclusions are to plausible violations.

26.10.2 A structured sensitivity analysis menu

Sensitivity analyses should not be ad hoc. The following menu provides a structured approach, with each tool appropriate for different aspects of the analysis:

1. Screening: E-values. The E-value quantifies the minimum strength of association that an unmeasured confounder would need to have with both treatment and outcome (on the risk ratio scale) to fully explain away an observed effect. It is a screening tool: a large E-value does not prove the absence of confounding, but a small E-value indicates that the finding is fragile. Report the E-value for the point estimate and for the confidence interval bound closest to the null.

2. Negative controls. A negative control outcome is an outcome known to be unaffected by the treatment. If the analysis detects an “effect” on the negative control, the method is likely biased (e.g., by residual confounding or selection bias). A negative control exposure is an exposure known not to affect the outcome; if it appears associated with the outcome after adjustment, the method has residual confounding. Negative controls do not eliminate bias, but they can detect it.

3. Quantitative bias analysis (QBA). Specify the parameters of a hypothetical unmeasured confounder: its prevalence in each treatment group and its association with the outcome. Then compute the bias-adjusted estimate under each scenario. This can be done analytically for simple confounders or via simulation for more complex structures. QBA transforms the qualitative statement “there might be unmeasured confounding” into a quantitative statement: “an unmeasured confounder with these specific properties would shift the estimate by this much.”

4. Design sensitivity. Vary key design decisions and assess robustness: - Time zero definition. Shift the time zero window (e.g., first dispensation vs. second dispensation) and re-estimate. - Grace period length. If a grace period is used for treatment strategy definition, vary its length (e.g., 30 days, 60 days, 90 days) and assess whether the estimate is stable. - Comparator choice. Compare results using different comparators (e.g., active comparator vs. non-user) to assess whether the comparison group drives the finding. - Covariate set. Add or remove covariates that are plausible confounders but potentially problematic (e.g., variables that might be on the causal pathway) and assess whether the estimate changes.

Avoid by: - Conducting quantitative bias analysis and sensitivity analyses for unmeasured confounding as a standard component of every analysis. - Using negative control outcomes or exposures when feasible. - Reporting E-values as a screening tool. - Varying design decisions (time zero, grace period, comparator, covariate set) when scientifically justified. - Reporting sensitivity analyses prominently, not in a supplementary appendix.

Diagnostics and sensitivity analysis. Report at least three of the following: E-value, negative control analysis, quantitative bias analysis, or design sensitivity analysis. State clearly how the conclusion changes (or does not) under each scenario. If a single sensitivity analysis would overturn the main finding, this must be acknowledged as a limitation.

26.11 Pitfall: Poor Alignment Between Randomized and Real-World Data in Hybrid Designs

Mistake: Pooling or comparing RCT and RWD without harmonizing definitions and populations.

Why it matters: Differences in eligibility, measurement, follow-up, and outcome definitions can dominate causal conclusions. Hybrid designs; which combine RCT data with external RWD controls, or use RWD to extend RCT follow-up; are increasingly common in regulatory submissions. When these data sources are not carefully harmonized, the “treatment effect” may reflect differences in data generation rather than differences in treatment.

RWE example. A single-arm trial of a new oncology drug uses external RWD controls from electronic health records. The trial enrolls patients at academic medical centers with strict eligibility criteria, ECOG performance status 0-1, and protocol-mandated imaging every 8 weeks. The RWD controls come from community oncology practices with broader patient populations, less frequent imaging, and outcomes ascertained from insurance claims. The apparent treatment benefit may reflect (a) healthier trial participants, (b) earlier outcome detection in the trial due to more frequent imaging, and (c) outcome misclassification in claims data, rather than a true drug effect.

Avoid by: - Harmonizing inclusion criteria, covariates, endpoints, and follow-up windows before analysis. Apply the RCT eligibility criteria to the RWD population to create a comparable external control group. - Checking covariate balance and comparability across data sources. Use standardized mean differences to assess whether the harmonized populations are similar on measured covariates. - Using principled external control and transportability approaches where applicable. Formal transportability methods re-weight the trial estimate to the target population using measured covariates, under the assumption that the measured covariates capture the relevant differences between populations. TMLE-based transportability estimators provide doubly robust estimation with valid inference. - Conducting negative control checks in RWD components. Apply the same analysis pipeline to an outcome known to be unaffected by the treatment. If the RWD analysis detects a spurious effect, the pipeline has residual bias.

Diagnostics and sensitivity analysis. Report covariate distributions in the trial and RWD populations before and after harmonization. Report standardized mean differences for all covariates. If transportability weights are used, report the weight distribution and effective sample size. As a sensitivity analysis, vary the set of covariates used for harmonization and assess stability. If a key covariate is available in the trial but not in the RWD (or vice versa), conduct a quantitative bias analysis for the missing confounder.

26.12 Pitfall: Reporting Without Context or Interpretation

Mistake: Reporting only a point estimate (or a single relative measure) without absolute risks, assumptions, or limitations.

Why it matters: Causal results are only meaningful relative to the estimand, assumptions, and the population. A risk ratio of 0.75 without context could mean a reduction from 40% to 30% (clinically important) or from 0.04% to 0.03% (clinically negligible). Without explicit statement of the estimand, the reader cannot assess what question the analysis answers. Without discussion of assumptions, the reader cannot assess the credibility of the answer. Without diagnostics, the reader cannot assess whether the analysis was well-executed.

RWE example. A manuscript reports that a new antidepressant “reduces suicidal ideation (OR = 0.68, 95% CI: 0.52-0.89, p = 0.005).” The reader is not told: (a) the baseline rate of suicidal ideation in the comparator group (is the absolute risk reduction 1% or 10%?), (b) the estimand (intention-to-treat analog? per-protocol?), (c) how treatment switching was handled (did patients who switched from the comparator to the new drug contribute to the comparator arm?), (d) whether positivity was adequate (were there patients in the data who would never receive the comparator?), or (e) whether any sensitivity analysis for unmeasured confounding was conducted.

Avoid by: - Reporting absolute risk estimates alongside contrasts (risk differences, survival probabilities) so that the clinical significance is apparent. - Transparently stating identification assumptions and their plausibility. At minimum: exchangeability (what confounders were adjusted for, and what might be missing), positivity (was overlap adequate?), and consistency (is the treatment version well-defined?). - Discussing limitations, diagnostics, and sensitivity analyses. - Framing results in terms of the hypothetical intervention and estimand, not in terms of the statistical model. - Using the reporting checklist provided at the end of this chapter.

Diagnostics and sensitivity analysis. Verify that the manuscript or report includes: (a) the estimand in plain language, (b) absolute risks in both arms, (c) the identification assumptions and their plausibility, (d) at least one diagnostic (e.g., propensity score overlap, weight distribution), and (e) at least one sensitivity analysis (e.g., E-value, negative control). If any of these are missing, the report is incomplete. As a robustness check, present results on multiple scales (risk difference, risk ratio, and number needed to treat) to assess whether the qualitative conclusion is consistent across measures.

26.13 Pitfall: Failure to Specify the Target Trial Protocol

26.13.1 Design-first failures leading to immortal time bias and related biases

Mistake: Proceeding directly to statistical modeling without first specifying the hypothetical randomized experiment that the observational analysis aims to emulate.

Why it matters: Most structural biases in pharmacoepidemiology; immortal time bias, selection bias from conditioning on the future, time-zero misalignment, prevalent-user bias; arise from failures at the design stage, not the estimation stage. When the target trial is not specified, analysts make implicit design decisions that often do not correspond to any coherent experiment. The result is an analysis that answers an ill-defined question, regardless of how sophisticated the estimator is.

Immortal time bias is the most common consequence. It arises when follow-up time is attributed to the treatment group during a period when the outcome could not have occurred or when the patient had not yet initiated treatment. In a well-specified target trial, this cannot happen: follow-up begins at time zero, which is the moment when eligibility is met and treatment strategy is assigned. If the observational emulation allows time zero to differ from treatment initiation; for example, by defining “treated” patients as those who eventually received the drug and backdating follow-up to cohort entry; the treated group accumulates outcome-free person-time that is not at risk, biasing the comparison in favor of treatment.

RWE example. A study of a new biologic for inflammatory bowel disease defines the exposed group as patients who received the biologic at any point during follow-up. Follow-up starts at diagnosis for all patients. Patients in the exposed group contribute months or years of person-time before biologic initiation during which they could not have an exposure-attributed event. This immortal time inflates the denominator of the exposed group’s event rate, making the drug appear protective even if it has no effect (or is harmful). The fix is to align time zero with biologic initiation, using a new-user design with an active comparator.

Avoid by: - Writing out the target trial protocol (using the checklist in the introduction) before any data analysis. The protocol forces explicit decisions about eligibility, time zero, treatment strategies, and outcome definitions. - Ensuring that time zero is the moment when eligibility is met AND the treatment strategy begins. Patients are followed from this point forward. There is no “pre-exposure” follow-up. - Using a new-user (incident-user) design to avoid prevalent-user bias (see Pitfall 15). - Verifying that no analysis decision depends on future information (i.e., information not available at time zero).

Diagnostics and sensitivity analysis. Draw a timeline for each treatment arm showing when eligibility is met, when treatment starts, and when follow-up begins. If these do not coincide, investigate whether immortal time bias is present. As a sensitivity analysis, re-estimate the effect under an alternative time zero definition (e.g., shifting time zero by 30, 60, or 90 days) and assess whether the estimate is stable. An estimate that changes dramatically with small shifts in time zero is likely contaminated by immortal time bias.

26.14 Pitfall: Misdefinition of Time Origin and Treatment Initiation

26.14.1 New-user versus prevalent-user misalignment

Mistake: Including patients who have already been using the treatment of interest at study entry (prevalent users), or failing to define a clear time zero for patients in the comparator group.

Why it matters: Prevalent-user bias arises because patients who are currently on treatment are a selected subgroup: they have tolerated the treatment, have not experienced early adverse events, and may have a different prognosis than patients who are just starting treatment. Including them biases the analysis toward the appearance of benefit (or, for safety studies, toward the appearance of safety) because the high-risk early period has already been survived.

For the comparator group, a parallel problem arises: if “non-users” are defined simply as patients who never receive the drug during follow-up, time zero is undefined. These patients may enter the analysis at any point; at diagnosis, at a clinic visit, or at some arbitrary calendar date; creating a heterogeneous comparator population with no clear moment of treatment assignment.

RWE example. A study compares patients “on metformin” (identified from prevalent prescriptions at a cross-sectional date) to patients “not on metformin.” The metformin group has been tolerating the drug for a median of three years and has survived any early side effects. The comparator group includes patients who may have never been candidates for metformin, patients who tried and stopped metformin years ago, and patients who would later initiate metformin. The comparison is not between starting metformin versus not starting metformin; it is between being an established metformin user versus a heterogeneous non-user population. This does not correspond to any well-defined intervention.

Avoid by: - Using a new-user (incident-user) design. Include only patients at the time of treatment initiation. Time zero is the date of first prescription, dispensation, or administration. - Defining an active comparator that provides a clear time zero for the comparator group. For example, compare initiators of Drug A with initiators of Drug B. Both groups have a well-defined time zero (the date of initiation), and both are starting a new treatment, reducing confounding by indication. - If a non-user comparator is necessary, define time zero using an analogous clinical event (e.g., the date of diagnosis, or the date of the clinical visit at which treatment candidacy is established) and ensure that eligibility criteria are applied at that date.

Diagnostics and sensitivity analysis. Report the distribution of prior treatment duration among “users” to verify that the cohort consists of new users. If some prevalent users are inadvertently included, assess sensitivity by restricting to patients with no prior prescriptions in progressively longer lookback windows (e.g., 6 months, 12 months, 24 months). If the estimate changes substantially with the lookback window, prevalent-user bias is likely present. As an additional check, plot the hazard function over time since treatment initiation. A decreasing hazard in the first weeks or months (the “depletion of susceptibles” pattern) suggests prevalent-user contamination or early-event selection.

26.15 Pitfall: Treatment Versioning and Ill-Defined Interventions

26.15.1 Dose, formulation, switching, and combination therapy

Mistake: Defining the treatment as a broad category (e.g., “SGLT2 inhibitor use”) without specifying dose, formulation, route, duration, or rules for treatment modifications.

Why it matters: The consistency assumption requires that the treatment variable maps to a well-defined intervention: if we say patient \(i\) received treatment \(A = 1\), we must be able to specify what that means in sufficient detail that the counterfactual outcome \(Y(1)\) is unambiguous. When “treatment” encompasses multiple doses, formulations, or treatment trajectories, the causal estimand is a weighted average over an undefined mixture of interventions, and the estimate may not correspond to any implementable policy.

RWE example. A study compares “statin users” to “non-users.” The statin group includes patients on low-dose atorvastatin 10 mg daily, high-dose rosuvastatin 40 mg daily, patients who started one statin and switched to another, and patients who took statins intermittently. The “effect of statin use” is an uninterpretable average over these heterogeneous treatment courses. A prescriber cannot act on the finding because it does not correspond to any specific prescribing decision.

Avoid by: - Specifying the treatment strategy in the target trial protocol with sufficient precision: the drug (or drug class, if a class effect is the target), dose range, formulation, route, intended duration, and rules for switching or dose adjustment. - When the intervention is inherently complex (e.g., a treatment algorithm with dose titration and switching rules), consider defining the strategy as a sustained treatment strategy (e.g., “initiate Drug A at dose X and continue for at least 6 months, with switching permitted only to Drug B if intolerance develops”). This is the treatment strategy that LTMLE and longitudinal MSMs can target. - When dose is continuous or varies over time, consider a modified treatment policy (MTP) that defines the intervention as a shift from the natural treatment value rather than a fixed dose. For example, “reduce daily opioid dose by 20% relative to the dose the patient would naturally receive.” - Reporting which treatment versions are included in the analysis and assessing effect modification by dose or formulation.

Diagnostics and sensitivity analysis. Tabulate the distribution of treatment versions (dose, formulation, duration) within the “treated” group. If the distribution is highly heterogeneous, assess whether the treatment effect varies across versions (e.g., estimate separately by dose category). As a sensitivity analysis, restrict the analysis to the most common treatment version and compare with the pooled estimate. If they differ, the pooled estimate is driven by treatment version composition, and the estimand should be refined.

26.16 Pitfall: Confusing Overlap Failure With a Modeling Problem

26.16.1 The estimand is not supported by the data

Mistake: Responding to positivity violations by changing the model (e.g., adding regularization, truncating weights more aggressively, using a different machine learning algorithm) rather than recognizing that the target estimand may not be identifiable from the available data.

Why it matters: When certain covariate strata have near-zero probability of receiving one treatment, the problem is not that the model is wrong; it is that the data do not contain the information needed to estimate the causal effect in those strata. No statistical method can create information that does not exist. Attempts to “fix” the problem by model adjustments (e.g., more aggressive weight truncation, stronger regularization of the propensity score) implicitly change the estimand without acknowledging it, because the effective target population shifts toward the region of overlap.

This pitfall is distinct from Pitfall 3, which describes the symptoms of positivity violations. The present pitfall addresses the common response of treating the problem as a modeling issue rather than an identifiability issue, and provides a decision framework for when to change the estimand rather than the estimator.

RWE example. A study compares surgical versus medical management of carotid stenosis. Patients over 85 with severe comorbidities are almost never offered surgery. The analyst observes extreme propensity score weights for these patients and responds by truncating weights at the 99th percentile. This truncation implicitly shifts the target population away from the elderly and severely ill patients; precisely the patients for whom the treatment comparison is most uncertain. The analyst reports the result as the ATE for the full population, but it is effectively an ATE for a restricted population defined by the truncation threshold.

26.16.2 When to restrict, repair, or report

  1. Restrict the population. If the positivity violation is concentrated in identifiable subgroups (e.g., patients over 85 with eGFR < 15), exclude them from the analysis and state the revised target population. This is honest and interpretable.

  2. Repair the estimand. If the scientific question permits, replace the static intervention with a stochastic intervention, incremental propensity score intervention, or modified treatment policy that does not require extrapolation beyond the observed data. These estimands are defined by interventions that respect the natural treatment probability rather than overriding it (see Pitfall 3 for details).

  3. Report the limitation. If neither restriction nor repair produces a scientifically meaningful alternative, state that the original estimand is not credibly identifiable and present the available evidence (e.g., estimated effects within the region of overlap) as partial information.

Avoid by: - Distinguishing between estimator instability (a modeling problem) and estimand non-identifiability (a data support problem). The test is conceptual: if the data-generating process guarantees that certain patients never receive one treatment, no model can estimate the treatment effect in those patients. - Using the structured diagnostic workflow from Pitfall 3 to characterize the nature and extent of positivity violations before choosing a response. - Reporting any truncation, restriction, or estimand change transparently, including the characteristics of excluded patients.

Diagnostics and sensitivity analysis. Report the propensity score distribution and identify the subgroups driving positivity violations. If weights are truncated, report the effective estimand (i.e., the population defined by the truncation threshold) and compare estimates at multiple truncation levels. As a sensitivity analysis, estimate the effect using a stochastic intervention with varying shift parameters (e.g., odds ratio shift of 1.5, 2.0, 3.0) and assess whether the direction and magnitude of the effect are stable across these alternative estimands.

26.17 Pitfall: Interference and Spillovers Ignored

26.17.1 Violated no-interference assumption

Mistake: Assuming that one patient’s treatment does not affect another patient’s outcome (the stable unit treatment value assumption, or SUTVA) in settings where interference is plausible.

Why it matters: The standard causal inference framework assumes no interference: the potential outcome for patient \(i\) depends only on patient \(i\)’s treatment, not on the treatments of other patients. When this assumption is violated; for example, in vaccine studies where vaccination of one person reduces infection risk for their contacts, or in hospital settings where antibiotic prescribing for one patient affects resistance patterns for other patients; the standard ATE is not well-defined, and methods that ignore interference can be substantially biased.

In pharmacoepidemiology, interference is less commonly discussed but can arise in several contexts: household-level exposures (e.g., secondhand smoke, shared medications), institutional prescribing patterns (e.g., hospital formulary decisions that affect all patients), community-level interventions (e.g., public health campaigns), and market-level effects (e.g., when widespread adoption of a drug changes the population of remaining non-users through selection).

RWE example. A study of influenza antiviral treatment estimates the effect of treating an index case on household transmission. The outcome for household contacts depends on whether the index case was treated (interference). Standard person-level IPTW applied to this setting would yield a biased estimate because it does not account for the fact that one patient’s treatment affects another’s outcome. The estimand must be defined at the household level (e.g., the effect of treating the index case on the probability that at least one household contact becomes infected).

Avoid by: - Assessing whether the no-interference assumption is scientifically plausible in the study setting. If patients share a household, institution, or prescribing physician, interference may be present. - When interference is plausible, defining the estimand at the cluster level (e.g., household, hospital, community) and using methods designed for interference, such as cluster-randomized trial analogs or inverse probability weighting with interference-aware estimands. - When interference is suspected but cannot be fully addressed, conducting a sensitivity analysis for the magnitude of spillover effects.

Diagnostics and sensitivity analysis. If the data contain cluster information (household, physician, hospital), test whether outcomes within clusters are correlated conditional on measured confounders, beyond what individual-level models predict. As a sensitivity analysis, re-estimate the treatment effect using cluster-robust standard errors and compare with individual-level standard errors. If cluster-robust standard errors are substantially larger, unmodeled within-cluster dependence (potentially due to interference) is inflating precision in the standard analysis.

26.18 Pitfall: Overstating External Validity Without Transportability Assessment

26.18.1 Generalizing beyond the study sample

Mistake: Interpreting a treatment effect estimated in one population as applicable to a different target population without formal assessment of transportability.

Why it matters: Every causal estimate is specific to the population in which it is identified. When the study population differs from the target population in ways that modify the treatment effect, applying the study estimate directly to the target population will be misleading. This is a concern both for extrapolating from RCTs (which typically enroll selected, healthier populations) and for extrapolating from one RWD source to another (e.g., from a claims database to a clinical registry).

Transportability requires that (a) the effect modifiers are measured in both the study and target populations, and (b) the treatment effect conditional on those modifiers is the same in both populations. Formal transportability methods re-weight or standardize the study estimate to the target population distribution of effect modifiers. Without this step, the analyst is implicitly assuming that the study population is representative of the target; an assumption that is rarely justified and should be assessed.

RWE example. A randomized trial of a new antihypertensive enrolls patients aged 40-70 with uncomplicated hypertension and no major comorbidities. The trial finds a 15% relative risk reduction in cardiovascular events. A health system wants to apply this estimate to its patient population, which includes many patients over 75 with diabetes, CKD, and heart failure. The treatment effect in this population may differ due to age-related pharmacokinetic changes, drug interactions, competing risks of death, and effect modification by comorbidity burden. Directly applying the trial estimate overstates the certainty of the expected benefit.

Avoid by: - Stating the target population explicitly in the estimand specification and assessing whether the study population is representative. - When the study and target populations differ, using formal transportability or generalizability methods to re-weight the study estimate. These methods require measurement of effect modifiers in both populations and use IPTW or TMLE to standardize the estimate to the target population. - When formal transportability is not feasible (e.g., because effect modifiers are not measured in the target population), acknowledging the limitation explicitly and discussing the direction and potential magnitude of the difference.

Diagnostics and sensitivity analysis. Compare the distribution of key effect modifiers between the study population and the target population. Compute standardized differences and assess whether the populations are comparable. If transportability weights are used, report the weight distribution and effective sample size. As a sensitivity analysis, vary the set of effect modifiers used for transportability adjustment and assess stability. If the transported estimate is sensitive to which modifiers are included, external validity is uncertain.

26.19 Pitfall: Inappropriate Handling of Competing Risks

26.19.1 When another event precludes the outcome of interest

Mistake: Applying standard survival analysis methods (Kaplan-Meier, Cox regression) to estimate the risk of an event in the presence of competing risks that preclude its occurrence, without acknowledging that the estimand has changed.

Why it matters: Standard Kaplan-Meier and Cox regression treat competing events as censoring. This implicitly estimates the event rate in a hypothetical world where the competing event does not exist; a quantity that may not be scientifically meaningful. For example, estimating the “risk of cardiac hospitalization” using Kaplan-Meier while censoring at non-cardiac death estimates the risk that would be observed if non-cardiac death were eliminated. This quantity is higher than the actual risk observed in the real world (where some patients die before they can be hospitalized) and may not correspond to any clinically actionable question.

RWE example. A study of hip fracture risk in elderly patients on bisphosphonates censors patients who die from any cause. In a population with high mortality (e.g., patients over 85 in long-term care), a substantial proportion die before a hip fracture can occur. Censoring at death inflates the estimated fracture risk because it describes the risk in a hypothetical world without mortality. The clinician, however, needs to know the actual probability of fracture in the real world where patients also die; i.e., the cumulative incidence accounting for the competing risk of death.

Avoid by: - Determining whether a competing event is present and, if so, defining the estimand accordingly. Use the competing risks decision rule from Pitfall 8: does the competing event change the meaning of the estimand? - If the goal is the real-world probability of the event (accounting for competing risks), use the cumulative incidence function (Aalen-Johansen estimator) or cause-specific subdistribution methods. - If the goal is the effect of treatment on the event-specific hazard (the rate among those still at risk), use cause-specific hazard models with the understanding that this conditions on survival and does not directly give the probability of the event. - For causal inference with competing risks, consider the separable effects framework, TMLE-based competing risk estimators (e.g., the concrete package), or defining a composite endpoint that includes both the event and the competing event.

Diagnostics and sensitivity analysis. Report the rate of the competing event by treatment arm. If competing events are common (e.g., >5% of the population), compare estimates from standard Kaplan-Meier (censoring at competing event) with cumulative incidence estimates (treating competing events as competing risks). If the two diverge substantially, the choice of handling method materially affects the conclusion. As a sensitivity analysis, define a composite endpoint (event + competing event) and estimate the treatment effect on the composite.

26.20 Pitfall: Measurement Error and Outcome Misclassification

26.20.1 Imperfect data do not produce perfect estimates

Mistake: Assuming that treatment, covariates, and outcomes are measured without error in administrative or electronic health record data.

Why it matters: Measurement error in treatment creates misclassification of the exposure, biasing the treatment effect toward the null (for non-differential misclassification) or in either direction (for differential misclassification). Measurement error in confounders produces residual confounding even when the true confounders are conceptually included in the model, because adjustment for a mismeasured confounder does not fully remove its confounding effect. Outcome misclassification can inflate or deflate event rates and bias treatment effect estimates.

In claims-based pharmacoepidemiology, all three forms of measurement error are common. Treatment exposure is typically measured by prescription dispensation, which may not reflect actual medication use (patients may not fill prescriptions, may fill but not take them, or may share medications). Confounders such as BMI, smoking status, or disease severity are often unavailable or measured with substantial error. Outcomes defined by diagnosis codes have imperfect sensitivity and specificity that varies across clinical settings and patient populations.

RWE example. A study of oral anticoagulant safety uses pharmacy dispensation records to define treatment exposure and ICD-10 codes to define bleeding outcomes. Patients who fill a prescription but do not take the medication are classified as exposed, diluting the apparent treatment effect. Bleeding events detected only through hospitalization codes miss outpatient bleeding events (imperfect sensitivity), while some coded bleeding events are rule-out diagnoses (imperfect specificity). Together, these measurement errors can attenuate or distort the estimated treatment effect.

Avoid by: - Validating treatment, covariate, and outcome definitions against chart review, linked clinical data, or published validation studies. Report the positive predictive value (PPV) and sensitivity of outcome definitions when available. - Conducting quantitative bias analysis for misclassification: specify the sensitivity and specificity of the outcome definition (or exposure definition) and compute the bias-adjusted estimate under a range of plausible misclassification parameters. - When confounder measurement error is suspected, using validation sub-studies or external calibration data to assess the magnitude of residual confounding due to mismeasurement. - Considering the use of probabilistic bias analysis that jointly models misclassification of exposure, outcome, and confounders.

Diagnostics and sensitivity analysis. Report the validation characteristics (PPV, sensitivity, specificity) of key variable definitions. If validation data are not available, cite published estimates from the same or similar data sources. Conduct a quantitative bias analysis assuming plausible ranges of sensitivity and specificity for the outcome definition (e.g., sensitivity 70-90%, specificity 95-99%) and report how the estimated treatment effect changes. If the conclusion is sensitive to misclassification parameters within the plausible range, this should be acknowledged as a limitation.

26.21 Reporting Checklist for Target Trial Emulations

Transparent reporting is essential for the credibility of causal analyses using observational data. The following checklist, aligned with emerging best practices for target trial emulation studies, should be used alongside the RECORD and STROBE guidelines.

Reporting checklist

Design

  1. Explicitly label the study as a target trial emulation.
  2. Present the target trial protocol (eligibility, time zero, treatment strategies, outcome, causal contrast, analysis plan) as a structured table.
  3. Present the observational emulation mapping: how each protocol element was implemented in the data source.

Estimand

  1. State the estimand using the estimand specification template (population, treatment, outcome, intercurrent event handling, summary measure).
  2. Distinguish the estimand from the statistical model. The estimand is the target; the model is the tool.

Identification and assumptions

  1. State the identification assumptions (exchangeability, positivity, consistency) and discuss their plausibility in the study context.
  2. Describe the confounder adjustment strategy and justify the covariate set with reference to a DAG or subject-matter knowledge.

Estimation

  1. Describe the estimator (g-computation, IPTW, TMLE, LTMLE, etc.) and link it to the estimand it targets.
  2. Describe nuisance model fitting (e.g., SuperLearner library, cross-fitting) and report cross-validated risk.

Diagnostics

  1. Report propensity score overlap and weight distributions (for IPTW/TMLE).
  2. Report covariate balance after weighting (SMDs).
  3. Report influence curve diagnostics and any observations with extreme influence (for TMLE).
  4. Report calibration of nuisance models.

Sensitivity analyses

  1. Report at least one sensitivity analysis for unmeasured confounding (E-value, quantitative bias analysis, or negative control).
  2. Report design sensitivity analyses (varying time zero, grace period, covariate set) as appropriate.
  3. State clearly how conclusions change (or do not change) under each sensitivity analysis.

Results

  1. Report absolute risks in each treatment arm alongside contrasts.
  2. Report results on at least one absolute scale (risk difference, survival probability, RMST) in addition to any relative measures.

26.22 Summary

Common pitfalls arise from misalignment between analytic choices and scientific questions, misspecification, unchecked assumptions, and overinterpretation. Avoiding them requires:

  • Explicit use of the Causal Roadmap
  • Diagnostics aligned with identification risks (for example, overlap and censoring)
  • Sensitivity analyses for plausible violations
  • Careful communication of estimands and assumptions

These practices improve credibility, reproducibility, and decision relevance.

The expanded set of pitfalls in this chapter can be distilled into the following ten principles:

Ten principles for credible causal inference in RWE

  1. Specify the target trial first. Write the target trial protocol; eligibility, time zero, treatment strategies, outcome, causal contrast; before touching the data. Most structural biases (immortal time, prevalent user, time-zero misalignment) are prevented by this step.

  2. Define the estimand before choosing the estimator. The estimand determines the identification conditions and the statistical functional. The estimator is chosen to estimate that functional. Reversing this order leads to analyses that answer unintended questions.

  3. Respect the temporal structure. Adjust only for pre-treatment covariates in the propensity score and baseline outcome model. If time-varying confounders are affected by prior treatment, use longitudinal methods (LTMLE, longitudinal MSMs). Never condition on post-treatment variables unless the estimand explicitly requires it.

  4. Diagnose positivity before estimating. Assess overlap through propensity score distributions, weight diagnostics, and effective sample sizes. When positivity fails, change the target population or the estimand; do not force an answer by truncating weights and pretending the estimand is unchanged.

  5. Use doubly robust estimation with flexible nuisance models and cross-fitting. TMLE and AIPW provide protection against misspecification of a single nuisance function. SuperLearner reduces dependence on parametric assumptions. Cross-fitting (CV-TMLE) guards against overfitting when the learner library includes highly adaptive algorithms.

  6. Choose estimands that match the decision context. Avoid defaulting to hazard ratios or odds ratios. Prefer risk differences, survival probabilities, cumulative incidence, or RMST; measures that are collapsible, causally interpretable, and clinically actionable. Handle competing risks explicitly.

  7. Treat sensitivity analysis as mandatory, not optional. Every observational analysis should include at least one assessment of robustness to unmeasured confounding (E-value, quantitative bias analysis, or negative control) and at least one design sensitivity analysis.

  8. Report transparently. State the estimand, the identification assumptions, the diagnostics, and the sensitivity analyses. Report absolute risks. Do not hide important limitations in supplementary materials.

  9. Distinguish identifiability from estimation. When the data cannot support the estimand (structural positivity violations, unmeasured confounders known to be important), no estimator can fix the problem. Acknowledge the limitation, restrict the population, or revise the estimand.

  10. Iterate. The Causal Roadmap is not linear. Diagnostics may reveal that the original estimand is not supported, requiring a return to the design stage. Sensitivity analyses may suggest that a different covariate set is needed. Treat this iteration as a feature of rigorous analysis, not a failure of planning.

# Common Pitfalls and How to Avoid Them
*A practical guide for avoiding frequent mistakes in causal inference and targeted learning*

Even with a solid understanding of the Causal Roadmap and modern estimators, it is easy to make analytic choices that lead to biased, unstable, or misleading results. In real-world evidence (RWE), the stakes are higher: data are messy, treatment pathways are irregular, and credibility depends on careful alignment between the scientific question, identification conditions, and the estimator.

This chapter catalogs common pitfalls encountered in causal inference, especially in pharmacoepidemiologic and longitudinal RWE settings, and provides practical strategies to avoid them. Each pitfall is organized around a common mistake, an explanation of why it matters, and concrete guidance for avoidance. Where possible, we connect the pitfall to the target trial emulation framework and the estimand, since most errors can be traced to a breakdown in the chain from question to design to identification to estimation.

---

## Pitfall: Confusing Association With Causation
### The regression coefficient problem

**Mistake:**
Fitting a multivariable regression and interpreting the treatment coefficient as a causal effect.

**Why it matters:**
Regression coefficients are typically **conditional associations**, not causal contrasts. Unless the model corresponds to the identified statistical functional and is correctly specified, the result may not estimate the causal estimand. In pharmacoepidemiology, this often manifests when an analyst fits a Cox model with treatment as a covariate alongside confounders and interprets the hazard ratio as the causal effect of treatment. The coefficient reflects neither the marginal causal effect nor any well-defined intervention, particularly when the model includes variables on the causal pathway or when the hazard ratio is non-collapsible.

**RWE example.** A study of statin initiation and cardiovascular events fits a multivariable Cox model adjusting for age, sex, LDL cholesterol, diabetes, and smoking status. The analyst reports the adjusted hazard ratio as the "effect of statins." However, if LDL is a mediator (statins lower LDL, which reduces cardiovascular risk), the coefficient does not correspond to the total causal effect. If the model is misspecified (e.g., the true relationship between age and the log-hazard is nonlinear), the coefficient is biased even for the conditional association.

**Avoid by:**
- Defining a precise **causal question** and **estimand** before fitting any model. Use the estimand specification template above.
- Estimating the corresponding statistical functional using **g-computation**, **IPTW**, **AIPW**, or **TMLE**, which explicitly separate the causal target from the nuisance estimation strategy.
- Using flexible nuisance estimation (for example, **SuperLearner**) where appropriate, so that the result does not depend on a single parametric specification.
- Drawing a DAG to determine which variables are confounders (must adjust), mediators (must not adjust for total effect), and colliders (must not condition on).

**Diagnostics and sensitivity analysis.**
Compare the regression-based estimate with a properly identified estimate (e.g., TMLE with the same covariate set). If the two diverge substantially, investigate whether model misspecification, non-collapsibility, or mediator adjustment explains the difference. As a robustness check, vary the functional form assumptions in the regression (e.g., add splines for continuous confounders) and assess stability.

## Pitfall: Adjusting for Post-Treatment Variables
### The mediator/collider trap

**Mistake:**
Including variables measured after treatment initiation in outcome regression or propensity score models.

**Why it matters:**
- Post-treatment variables may lie on the **causal pathway** (mediators), so adjustment can block part of the effect
- They may be **colliders**, inducing spurious associations
- Adjustment can invalidate the intervention being emulated

This pitfall is directly connected to the target trial protocol. In a well-specified target trial, the adjustment set consists of baseline covariates measured at or before time zero. Any variable measured after time zero is, by definition, post-treatment and should not appear in the propensity score or baseline outcome model unless the analysis explicitly targets a mediation estimand or uses longitudinal methods designed for time-varying confounding.

**RWE example.** A study of biologic therapy for rheumatoid arthritis adjusts for C-reactive protein (CRP) measured three months after treatment initiation. CRP is affected by treatment (biologics reduce inflammation) and also predicts joint destruction (the outcome). Adjusting for three-month CRP blocks the indirect pathway through inflammation reduction, attenuating the estimated total effect. If the analyst instead wants the direct effect not mediated by CRP, that is a different estimand requiring a different identification strategy (e.g., natural direct effect estimation via mediation analysis).

**Decision rule for time-varying confounding.** If a covariate is (a) affected by prior treatment and (b) also a confounder of subsequent treatment and the outcome, then baseline-only adjustment is insufficient. This is the hallmark of **time-varying confounding affected by prior treatment**, and it requires longitudinal methods: IPTW for marginal structural models, longitudinal g-computation, or LTMLE. These methods adjust for the confounding without blocking the causal pathway, by modeling the treatment and censoring mechanisms at each time point rather than conditioning on intermediate values.

**Avoid by:**
- Restricting adjustment to **baseline** confounders for baseline treatment effects, as specified in the target trial protocol.
- Using longitudinal methods (for example, **LTMLE** or **LMTP**) when time-varying confounding affected by prior treatment is present. LTMLE targets the G-formula under sequential exchangeability, estimating the counterfactual outcome under sustained treatment strategies while respecting the temporal ordering of confounders, treatments, and outcomes.
- Drawing DAGs to clarify temporal ordering and avoid collider bias.
- When estimating a **per-protocol effect** (the effect of sustained adherence), treating adherence as part of the treatment strategy and using IPCW or LTMLE to adjust for post-baseline confounders of adherence, rather than simply conditioning on adherence status.

**Diagnostics and sensitivity analysis.**
Examine whether any covariates in the adjustment set were measured after time zero. If time-varying confounders are present, compare estimates from baseline-only adjustment with those from IPTW or LTMLE to quantify the magnitude of time-varying confounding bias. As a sensitivity analysis, assess how results change when suspected mediators are removed from or added to the adjustment set.

## Pitfall: Violated Positivity or Limited Overlap
### Treatment not comparable across covariate strata

**Symptoms:**
- Propensity scores close to 0 or 1
- Large or highly variable IPTW weights
- Extreme clever covariate values in TMLE

**Why it matters:**
Positivity violations can lead to instability, high variance, and in finite samples, estimand drift toward extrapolation. But the consequences extend beyond estimator performance. Positivity is an **identifiability condition**: if certain covariate strata contain patients who would never receive one of the treatment arms, the causal effect is not nonparametrically identified in those strata. No amount of modeling flexibility can recover information that the data do not contain. This is "practical positivity"; a statement about the data-generating process, not merely about the estimator.

**RWE example.** A study compares warfarin to direct oral anticoagulants (DOACs) for stroke prevention in atrial fibrillation. Patients aged over 90 with severe renal impairment almost never receive DOACs in the data (probability near zero). The ATE for this subgroup is not identifiable from the observed data. Including these patients inflates weights, destabilizes variance, and shifts the estimate toward extrapolation. The analyst must decide whether to restrict the population, change the estimand, or report the limitation.

### Structured overlap diagnostic workflow

Positivity assessment should follow a structured sequence, not a single plot.

1. **Propensity score overlap plot.** Plot the distribution of estimated propensity scores by treatment group (e.g., mirrored histograms or density plots). Look for regions where one group has near-zero density.

2. **Weight distribution.** Compute stabilized IPTW weights and examine the distribution: maximum weight, ratio of maximum to median, and the proportion of total weight carried by the top 1% of observations. If a small number of observations carry a large share of the total weight, the estimate is fragile.

3. **Effective sample size (ESS).** Calculate $\text{ESS} = (\sum w_i)^2 / \sum w_i^2$ for each treatment group. If the ESS is substantially smaller than the actual sample size (e.g., less than half), the analysis is relying heavily on a small number of reweighted observations.

4. **Sensitivity to truncation.** Estimate the treatment effect at several truncation thresholds (e.g., truncating weights at the 95th, 99th, and 99.5th percentiles). If the point estimate shifts substantially with truncation, the result depends on the tails of the weight distribution; a sign that the estimand may not be well-supported.

### Decision rule for positivity failures

When diagnostics reveal positivity problems, the analyst faces three options, in order of preference:

1. **Restrict the population to common support.** Exclude covariate strata where one treatment arm has near-zero probability. This changes the target population (and therefore the estimand) to one that is identifiable. Report the restriction transparently.

2. **Change the estimand to a support-aware alternative.** When static interventions ("set treatment to 1 for everyone") violate positivity, consider estimands defined by interventions that respect the natural treatment assignment:
   - **Stochastic interventions** define a policy that shifts the probability of treatment rather than setting it deterministically. For example, "increase the probability of DOAC initiation by 20% for each patient." These estimands are identified even when some patients have near-zero probability of one treatment, because the intervention does not force treatment where it would not naturally occur.
   - **Incremental propensity score interventions** multiply the odds of treatment by a fixed factor (e.g., doubling the odds of treatment for everyone). These provide a smooth, interpretable contrast that avoids extrapolation.
   - **Modified treatment policies (MTPs)** define the intervention as a function of the natural value of treatment. For continuous or time-varying exposures, an MTP might specify "reduce dose by 10% at each time point relative to the dose the patient would naturally receive." The `lmtp` package implements longitudinal MTPs with TMLE-based estimation.

   Redefining the estimand is not a concession of failure. It is often the correct scientific response to a structural feature of the data-generating process. If the data cannot support the original question, asking a closely related question that the data can answer is more informative than forcing an unstable answer to the original one.

3. **Report that the original estimand is not credibly identifiable.** If neither restriction nor estimand modification produces a scientifically meaningful question, state explicitly that the data do not support the intended causal comparison. This is a legitimate and important finding.

**Avoid by:**
- Inspecting propensity score overlap and weight distributions using the workflow above.
- Truncating or stabilizing weights when justified, and reporting sensitivity to truncation level.
- Restricting to **regions of common support** when positivity violations are concentrated in identifiable subgroups.
- Considering **stochastic interventions** or **modified treatment policies** when static interventions are not feasible in the observed data.

**Diagnostics and sensitivity analysis.**
Report the propensity score overlap plot, weight distribution summary statistics (max, ESS, proportion of weight in top 1%), and estimates under at least two truncation thresholds. If restricting the population, report the characteristics of excluded patients and discuss how the restriction changes the target population. Conduct a sensitivity analysis comparing the ATE on the full population with the ATE on the restricted population and, if feasible, with a stochastic intervention estimand.

## Pitfall: Misspecified Outcome or Propensity Models
### Relying on simple models in complex settings

**Mistake:**
Assuming linearity, additivity, or simple parametric relationships without support.

**Why it matters:**
Misspecification of nuisance functions can create bias, especially for non-collapsible estimands or when overlap is limited. For IPTW, misspecification of the propensity score model leads to biased weights and biased treatment effect estimates. For g-computation, misspecification of the outcome model directly biases the estimate. TMLE and AIPW offer double robustness; consistency if either the outcome model or the treatment model is correctly specified; but this protection is only asymptotic and depends on regularity conditions. In finite samples with substantial misspecification of both models, even doubly robust estimators can be biased.

**RWE example.** A study of metformin initiation and HbA1c reduction fits a linear outcome model adjusting for age, BMI, baseline HbA1c, and eGFR as main effects. However, the relationship between baseline HbA1c and the outcome is strongly nonlinear (patients with higher baseline HbA1c have more room for reduction), and there is an interaction between BMI and metformin effect. The linear model misses both features. When the outcome model is used in g-computation, the estimated ATE is biased. When used as the initial estimate in TMLE, the targeting step can partially correct the bias if the propensity score model is well-specified, but the correction is incomplete if the propensity score model is also too rigid.

**Avoid by:**
- Using **SuperLearner** or other data-adaptive estimation with cross-validation to reduce dependence on a single parametric specification. SuperLearner combines multiple candidate learners (e.g., generalized linear models, random forests, gradient boosting, multivariate adaptive regression splines) using cross-validated risk to select the optimal weighted combination.
- Inspecting calibration and predictive performance (for example, cross-validated risk, calibration plots for propensity scores).
- Including domain-informed learners alongside flexible algorithms when strong structure is known (e.g., a parametric learner that includes a known interaction, alongside data-adaptive learners that can discover additional structure).

**Diagnostics and sensitivity analysis.**
Report the cross-validated risk of the SuperLearner ensemble and the relative contribution of each candidate learner. For propensity scores, produce calibration plots (predicted probability versus observed treatment proportion in deciles). Compare estimates from a simple parametric model with estimates from SuperLearner-based TMLE. If the two diverge, investigate which model features drive the difference. As a robustness check, vary the SuperLearner library (e.g., add or remove highly flexible learners) and assess stability of the treatment effect estimate.

## Pitfall: Blindly Trusting Machine Learning
### Flexible models do not guarantee valid inference

**Mistake:**
Assuming that a strong predictive model implies a correct causal estimate.

**Why it matters:**
Machine learning can overfit, extrapolate, or yield poorly calibrated probabilities. Prediction quality alone does not ensure correct estimation of a causal functional. A model that achieves excellent cross-validated AUC for treatment prediction is not necessarily a good propensity score model for causal inference: the model may be well-calibrated on average but poorly calibrated in the tails, precisely where causal estimation is most sensitive. Furthermore, a highly flexible learner fit on the full sample can produce plug-in bias; the nuisance parameter estimate overfits to the sample in a way that biases the final causal estimate, even asymptotically, unless proper sample-splitting is used.

### Prediction versus causal estimation

The objective in nuisance estimation is not to maximize prediction accuracy in the population. It is to obtain consistent estimates of nuisance functions (the outcome regression and the treatment/censoring mechanisms) that, when plugged into the causal functional, yield a consistent and efficient estimate of the target parameter. A model that is overfit to the training data will produce nuisance estimates that are too responsive to sample-specific noise. When these are plugged into the TMLE or AIPW estimator, the result inherits that noise, producing standard errors that are too narrow and confidence intervals with incorrect coverage.

### Cross-fitting as a guardrail

**Cross-fitting** (also called sample splitting) partitions the data into K folds. For each fold, nuisance functions are estimated on the remaining K-1 folds and evaluated on the held-out fold. The causal parameter is then estimated using the cross-fitted nuisance predictions. This eliminates the Donsker-class condition that would otherwise be required for consistency when using highly adaptive learners (e.g., random forests, boosted trees, neural networks). Cross-validated TMLE (CV-TMLE) implements this approach and should be preferred whenever the SuperLearner library includes learners that are more complex than parametric regression.

### ML leakage checklist

Before fitting any machine learning model for nuisance estimation, verify:

- **No future information.** Features used to predict treatment at time $t$ must be measured at or before time $t$. Features used to predict the outcome must not include post-treatment variables unless the estimand explicitly requires them (e.g., mediation).
- **No post-baseline variables in the propensity score model** unless the analysis uses a longitudinal framework (LTMLE) that explicitly models the time-varying treatment mechanism.
- **No biased feature construction.** Derived variables (e.g., "number of prior hospitalizations") must not inadvertently encode outcome information or use look-ahead data.
- **No outcome information in the treatment model.** The propensity score model must not include the outcome or variables determined after the outcome.

### Targeted learning diagnostics

When using TMLE or AIPW, the following diagnostics help assess whether nuisance estimation is adequate for causal inference:

**Clever covariate extremes.** In TMLE, the clever covariate $H(A, W) = I(A=1)/g(W) - I(A=0)/(1-g(W))$ drives the targeting step. When propensity scores $g(W)$ are near 0 or 1, the clever covariate becomes extreme, and the targeting step can produce large, unstable updates. Inspect the distribution of clever covariate values. If a small number of observations have values orders of magnitude larger than the rest, those observations dominate the targeting step and may destabilize the estimate.

**Influence curve stability.** The efficient influence curve (EIC) provides observation-level contributions to the parameter estimate. Compute the EIC for each observation and inspect the distribution. Observations with EIC values far from the mean (e.g., beyond 5 standard deviations) exert disproportionate influence. Investigate whether these correspond to positivity violations, extreme covariate values, or data errors.

**Collaborative targeting.** When nuisance estimation is challenging (e.g., rare outcomes, near-violations of positivity), standard TMLE may benefit from **collaborative targeted learning** approaches that jointly optimize the outcome and treatment models to minimize the variance of the target parameter, rather than optimizing each model independently. This is conceptually similar to choosing the propensity score model that produces the most efficient TMLE, rather than the one that best predicts treatment. When standard diagnostics reveal instability, consider whether collaborative targeting or regularization of the targeting step improves performance.

**Avoid by:**
- Using strict **cross-validation** and cross-fitting (CV-TMLE) when the learner library includes flexible algorithms.
- Checking calibration (especially for propensity scores) and inspecting clever covariate distributions.
- Using TMLE or other doubly robust approaches to reduce sensitivity to nuisance estimation errors, subject to regularity conditions.
- Applying the ML leakage checklist before model fitting.

**Diagnostics and sensitivity analysis.**
Report cross-validated risk for each nuisance model, propensity score calibration plots, the distribution of clever covariate values, and a summary of influence curve extremes (e.g., number of observations with EIC values beyond $\pm 3$ or $\pm 5$ standard deviations of the mean). As a sensitivity analysis, compare estimates from CV-TMLE with estimates from non-cross-fitted TMLE. If they differ substantially, overfitting of nuisance models is likely a concern.

## Pitfall: Ignoring Censoring and Informative Dropout

**Mistake:**
Treating censoring as non-informative without justification, or ignoring treatment discontinuation and switching when they affect the estimand.

**Why it matters:**
Informative censoring can bias estimates of risk and survival functionals. In pharmacoepidemiology, censoring is rarely truly random. Patients who discontinue treatment, switch to a competitor drug, transfer care, or die from competing causes are typically different from those who remain under observation. If the factors driving censoring also affect the outcome (informative censoring), naive analyses that treat censored observations as non-informative will be biased.

The connection to the estimand is critical. Whether treatment discontinuation or switching constitutes "censoring" or is part of the estimand depends on the causal question. Under a **treatment-policy (intention-to-treat analog) estimand**, switching is part of the natural course and patients are followed regardless. Under a **per-protocol estimand**, deviation from the assigned strategy is an intercurrent event that must be handled; either through censoring at deviation and IPCW adjustment, or through a hypothetical estimand that models the counterfactual under sustained adherence.

**RWE example.** A study compares two antihypertensive regimens for a composite cardiovascular endpoint over five years. Twenty percent of patients in one arm switch to the other within two years, and switching is more common among patients with poorly controlled blood pressure (a predictor of the outcome). Under a per-protocol estimand, naive censoring at switch removes the highest-risk patients from one arm, biasing the estimate toward the null. Under a treatment-policy estimand, all patients are followed regardless of switching, but the estimate reflects the real-world treatment trajectory (including switching) rather than the pharmacological effect of sustained therapy.

**Avoid by:**
- Defining censoring relative to the estimand in the target trial protocol. Distinguish administrative censoring (end of data availability, unrelated to health status) from informative censoring (loss to follow-up, switching, competing events).
- Modeling censoring mechanisms (for example, with SuperLearner) and applying **IPCW** when the censoring mechanism depends on measured covariates.
- Using TMLE/LTMLE with explicit censoring nodes in longitudinal settings. LTMLE jointly models the treatment, censoring, and outcome processes at each time point, providing doubly robust estimation under sequential exchangeability.
- Specifying how adherence and switching are handled in the estimand. Switching can be part of the treatment strategy (treatment-policy), a censoring event (per-protocol with IPCW), or the basis of a hypothetical estimand (what would have happened if no one switched). The choice determines the analysis method.

**Diagnostics and sensitivity analysis.**
Report the rate and timing of censoring by treatment arm and by key baseline covariates. If IPCW is used, report the distribution of censoring weights and the effective sample size. Compare estimates from (a) naive complete-case analysis, (b) IPCW-adjusted analysis, and (c) LTMLE with censoring nodes. As a sensitivity analysis, assess how the estimate changes under plausible violations of the independent censoring assumption: for example, assume that censored patients have 20%, 50%, or 100% higher event rates than non-censored patients with the same covariates, and bound the resulting bias.

## Pitfall: Over-interpreting Heterogeneous Treatment Effects
### Subgroup and HTE analyses are fragile

**Mistake:**
Treating exploratory subgroup findings as confirmatory evidence.

**Why it matters:**
HTE estimates are often high variance, subject to multiple testing, and sensitive to modeling choices. In pharmacoepidemiology, it is common to examine treatment effects within subgroups defined by age, sex, comorbidity, or biomarker status. The combination of many potential subgroups with small subgroup-specific sample sizes creates a high risk of spurious findings. A treatment that appears beneficial in patients with CKD but harmful in patients without CKD may simply reflect statistical noise, particularly if the subgroup analysis was not pre-specified.

The problem is compounded when data-adaptive methods are used to discover subgroups. Causal forests, for example, can identify regions of the covariate space with heterogeneous treatment effects, but the resulting estimates are inherently exploratory. The same flexibility that allows the algorithm to detect heterogeneity also allows it to find patterns that do not replicate. Without external validation or a confirmatory design, subgroup-specific treatment effects discovered by machine learning should be treated as hypotheses, not conclusions.

**RWE example.** A study of a new anticoagulant reports that the drug reduces stroke in patients over 75 but increases bleeding in patients under 65. The subgroup analysis was conducted post hoc across 12 subgroups defined by age, sex, renal function, and concomitant medications. After accounting for multiple comparisons, neither finding reaches conventional significance. The headline finding of differential effects by age is likely an artifact of multiple testing.

**Avoid by:**
- Pre-specifying subgroups and hypotheses in the analysis plan, ideally motivated by biological plausibility or prior evidence.
- Reporting uncertainty and accounting for multiplicity where relevant. At minimum, report confidence intervals for subgroup-specific effects and note the number of subgroups examined.
- Treating causal forest or related approaches as exploratory unless paired with confirmatory design (e.g., prospective validation in independent data).
- Using targeted-learning-based approaches (for example, TMLE-based variable importance) when aligned with the scientific objective. These approaches can provide valid inference for pre-specified effect modification questions while using flexible modeling for nuisance estimation.

**Diagnostics and sensitivity analysis.**
Report the number of subgroups examined and whether they were pre-specified. For data-adaptive HTE methods, report cross-validated estimates and honest confidence intervals (e.g., from honest causal forests). As a sensitivity analysis, assess whether the identified heterogeneity pattern is robust to changes in the learner library, the covariate set, or the definition of subgroups. If a finding is not robust to reasonable analytic variations, it should be presented as preliminary.

## Pitfall: Using the Wrong Estimand
### Odds ratios and hazard ratios are commonly misinterpreted

**Mistake:**
Defaulting to odds ratios or hazard ratios and interpreting them as causal effects without careful justification.

**Why it matters:**
- Odds ratios are non-collapsible and may not correspond to the causal contrast of interest. A conditional odds ratio from a logistic regression generally differs from the marginal odds ratio even when there is no confounding, because the odds ratio does not average linearly across strata.
- Hazard ratios are often difficult to interpret causally in the presence of time-varying hazards and selection into the risk set. Even in a randomized trial, the hazard ratio at time $t$ conditions on survival to time $t$, which is affected by prior treatment. If treatment affects early survival, the risk sets at later times are no longer comparable between arms, and the hazard ratio reflects both the treatment effect and the selection of survivors. This built-in selection bias means that hazard ratios do not have a clean causal interpretation in most settings.

### Why hazard ratios are problematic as causal estimands

The Cox proportional hazards model estimates a hazard ratio under the assumption that the ratio of hazard functions is constant over time. When this assumption holds, the hazard ratio provides a compact summary. However:

1. **Non-collapsibility.** The conditional hazard ratio (adjusted for covariates) generally differs from the marginal hazard ratio, even when the covariates are not confounders. This is an intrinsic property of the hazard ratio as a summary measure, not a sign of confounding.
2. **Built-in selection bias.** At any time $t$, the hazard ratio compares event rates among patients who have survived to time $t$. If treatment affects survival before time $t$, the surviving populations in the two arms differ in ways that may not be fully captured by measured covariates. The hazard ratio at time $t$ therefore reflects both the treatment effect and this differential survival selection.
3. **Non-proportionality.** When hazards are non-proportional (as is common in pharmacoepidemiology, e.g., early benefit that wanes, or delayed treatment effects), a single hazard ratio is misleading as a summary.

### Preferred alternatives for survival outcomes

- **Survival probability at clinically meaningful time points** (e.g., 1-year survival, 5-year survival). Directly interpretable and can be estimated with g-computation, IPTW, or TMLE.
- **Risk difference or risk ratio at a fixed time horizon** (e.g., 90-day risk difference). Provides an absolute or relative measure on a scale that clinicians and patients can act on.
- **Restricted mean survival time (RMST)** to a specified time horizon $\tau$. The RMST difference gives the expected gain or loss in event-free survival time over the interval $[0, \tau]$. It does not require proportional hazards and is interpretable even when hazard ratios are not.
- **Cumulative incidence under competing risks.** When a competing event (e.g., death from non-cardiac causes) can preclude the outcome of interest (e.g., cardiac hospitalization), the cause-specific hazard approach conditions on surviving the competing event, which may not reflect a scientifically meaningful quantity. The cumulative incidence function (or sub-distribution hazard via Fine-Gray) accounts for the competing event, but care is needed: the estimand is the probability of the event in the real world where the competing event also occurs, not the probability in a world without competing events.

### Competing risks decision rule

When a competing event is present, determine whether it:

1. **Changes the meaning of the estimand.** If the competing event (e.g., death) precludes the outcome, the cause-specific hazard estimates the rate of the outcome among those still alive; a quantity that conditions on an intermediate event affected by treatment. This is often not the estimand of interest.
2. **Should be incorporated into the estimand.** Options include the cumulative incidence (probability of the event by time $t$ in the presence of the competing risk), a composite endpoint (event or competing event, whichever comes first), or a treatment-policy approach that follows all patients regardless of competing events (using, e.g., the separable effects framework or total effect on a composite).
3. **Requires its own sensitivity analysis.** Vary the handling of the competing event (e.g., compare cause-specific analysis with competing-risk analysis) and assess how conclusions change.

**Avoid by:**
- Targeting risk differences, risk ratios, survival curves, cumulative incidence, or RMST when scientifically appropriate. State the estimand explicitly using the estimand specification template.
- Using methods designed for causal survival analysis (including targeted learning approaches, the `concrete` package for competing risks, or LTMLE for time-to-event outcomes) when time-to-event outcomes are primary.
- Reporting hazard ratios only as supplementary measures, with explicit caveats about non-collapsibility and selection bias, and always alongside absolute risk measures.

**Diagnostics and sensitivity analysis.**
Test the proportional hazards assumption (e.g., Schoenfeld residuals, log-log survival plots). If non-proportionality is detected, report time-varying hazard ratios or, preferably, switch to RMST or survival probability contrasts. When competing risks are present, compare cause-specific and competing-risk analyses and report how the estimate changes. As a sensitivity analysis, vary the time horizon for risk difference or RMST estimation to assess whether conclusions are robust to the choice of follow-up window.

## Pitfall: Not Performing Diagnostics
### "The model ran" does not imply validity

**Mistake:**
Reporting results without checking whether key assumptions are approximately supported in the observed data.

**Why it matters:**
Many failures in applied causal inference arise from detectable issues such as overlap problems, extreme weights, or unstable influence curves. The danger is not that these problems are invisible; it is that analysts often skip the diagnostics that would reveal them. In targeted learning, the EIC provides a built-in diagnostic: if the estimated EIC has extreme values, the estimate is driven by a small number of observations, and the standard error may understate the true uncertainty. In IPTW, the weight distribution directly reveals whether the analysis is well-supported by the data or is extrapolating from a handful of reweighted observations.

**RWE example.** A TMLE analysis of a new diabetes medication reports a risk difference of -0.04 (95% CI: -0.06 to -0.02), suggesting a 4-percentage-point reduction in cardiovascular events. However, inspection of the influence curve reveals that three observations (out of 50,000) have EIC values that are 50 times the standard deviation of the remaining observations. These three patients have propensity scores below 0.01, and the clever covariate drives the targeting step to assign them enormous influence. The reported confidence interval does not account for this instability. After removing these observations (or equivalently, truncating the propensity score at 0.01), the estimate shifts to -0.02 with a wider confidence interval. The original result was not robust.

### A diagnostic checklist for causal analyses

1. **Propensity score overlap.** Plot distributions by treatment group. Flag regions with near-zero density.
2. **Weight distributions.** Report max, mean, median, ESS, and the proportion of total weight in the top 1% of observations.
3. **Clever covariate distribution** (TMLE-specific). Report the range and identify extreme values. Values orders of magnitude larger than the median indicate near-positivity violations.
4. **Influence curve distribution.** Compute the EIC for each observation. Report the standard deviation and the number of observations beyond $\pm 3$ and $\pm 5$ SDs. Investigate outliers.
5. **Nuisance model calibration.** For the propensity score, produce calibration plots (decile-based observed vs. predicted treatment probability). For the outcome model, report cross-validated risk and, if possible, calibration in relevant subgroups.
6. **Covariate balance after weighting.** Compute standardized mean differences (SMDs) for all covariates, before and after weighting. Residual imbalance (SMD > 0.1) after weighting suggests misspecification of the propensity score model.

**Avoid by:**
- Inspecting weight distributions and effective sample sizes.
- Checking clever covariate ranges and outliers in targeted learning.
- Examining influence curve stability and identifying influential observations.
- Evaluating nuisance model calibration and cross-validated risk.
- Treating diagnostics as a required component of the analysis, not an optional supplement.

**Diagnostics and sensitivity analysis.**
Report all items from the diagnostic checklist above. As a sensitivity analysis, re-estimate the treatment effect after removing the most influential observations (e.g., top 0.1% by absolute EIC value) and assess stability. If the estimate changes substantially, the result depends on a small number of observations and should be interpreted with caution.

## Pitfall: Neglecting Sensitivity Analyses
### Unmeasured confounding is not solved by optimism

**Mistake:**
Assuming exchangeability holds without probing robustness.

**Why it matters:**
In observational settings, residual confounding is frequently plausible and may change qualitative conclusions. No design or analytic approach can eliminate unmeasured confounding from observational data. The best an analyst can do is (a) make the no unmeasured confounding assumption as plausible as possible through careful design and covariate selection, and (b) assess how sensitive the conclusions are to plausible violations.

### A structured sensitivity analysis menu

Sensitivity analyses should not be ad hoc. The following menu provides a structured approach, with each tool appropriate for different aspects of the analysis:

**1. Screening: E-values.** The E-value quantifies the minimum strength of association that an unmeasured confounder would need to have with both treatment and outcome (on the risk ratio scale) to fully explain away an observed effect. It is a screening tool: a large E-value does not prove the absence of confounding, but a small E-value indicates that the finding is fragile. Report the E-value for the point estimate and for the confidence interval bound closest to the null.

**2. Negative controls.** A negative control outcome is an outcome known to be unaffected by the treatment. If the analysis detects an "effect" on the negative control, the method is likely biased (e.g., by residual confounding or selection bias). A negative control exposure is an exposure known not to affect the outcome; if it appears associated with the outcome after adjustment, the method has residual confounding. Negative controls do not eliminate bias, but they can detect it.

**3. Quantitative bias analysis (QBA).** Specify the parameters of a hypothetical unmeasured confounder: its prevalence in each treatment group and its association with the outcome. Then compute the bias-adjusted estimate under each scenario. This can be done analytically for simple confounders or via simulation for more complex structures. QBA transforms the qualitative statement "there might be unmeasured confounding" into a quantitative statement: "an unmeasured confounder with these specific properties would shift the estimate by this much."

**4. Design sensitivity.** Vary key design decisions and assess robustness:
- **Time zero definition.** Shift the time zero window (e.g., first dispensation vs. second dispensation) and re-estimate.
- **Grace period length.** If a grace period is used for treatment strategy definition, vary its length (e.g., 30 days, 60 days, 90 days) and assess whether the estimate is stable.
- **Comparator choice.** Compare results using different comparators (e.g., active comparator vs. non-user) to assess whether the comparison group drives the finding.
- **Covariate set.** Add or remove covariates that are plausible confounders but potentially problematic (e.g., variables that might be on the causal pathway) and assess whether the estimate changes.

**Avoid by:**
- Conducting quantitative bias analysis and sensitivity analyses for unmeasured confounding as a standard component of every analysis.
- Using negative control outcomes or exposures when feasible.
- Reporting E-values as a screening tool.
- Varying design decisions (time zero, grace period, comparator, covariate set) when scientifically justified.
- Reporting sensitivity analyses prominently, not in a supplementary appendix.

**Diagnostics and sensitivity analysis.**
Report at least three of the following: E-value, negative control analysis, quantitative bias analysis, or design sensitivity analysis. State clearly how the conclusion changes (or does not) under each scenario. If a single sensitivity analysis would overturn the main finding, this must be acknowledged as a limitation.

## Pitfall: Poor Alignment Between Randomized and Real-World Data in Hybrid Designs

**Mistake:**
Pooling or comparing RCT and RWD without harmonizing definitions and populations.

**Why it matters:**
Differences in eligibility, measurement, follow-up, and outcome definitions can dominate causal conclusions. Hybrid designs; which combine RCT data with external RWD controls, or use RWD to extend RCT follow-up; are increasingly common in regulatory submissions. When these data sources are not carefully harmonized, the "treatment effect" may reflect differences in data generation rather than differences in treatment.

**RWE example.** A single-arm trial of a new oncology drug uses external RWD controls from electronic health records. The trial enrolls patients at academic medical centers with strict eligibility criteria, ECOG performance status 0-1, and protocol-mandated imaging every 8 weeks. The RWD controls come from community oncology practices with broader patient populations, less frequent imaging, and outcomes ascertained from insurance claims. The apparent treatment benefit may reflect (a) healthier trial participants, (b) earlier outcome detection in the trial due to more frequent imaging, and (c) outcome misclassification in claims data, rather than a true drug effect.

**Avoid by:**
- Harmonizing inclusion criteria, covariates, endpoints, and follow-up windows before analysis. Apply the RCT eligibility criteria to the RWD population to create a comparable external control group.
- Checking covariate balance and comparability across data sources. Use standardized mean differences to assess whether the harmonized populations are similar on measured covariates.
- Using principled external control and transportability approaches where applicable. Formal transportability methods re-weight the trial estimate to the target population using measured covariates, under the assumption that the measured covariates capture the relevant differences between populations. TMLE-based transportability estimators provide doubly robust estimation with valid inference.
- Conducting negative control checks in RWD components. Apply the same analysis pipeline to an outcome known to be unaffected by the treatment. If the RWD analysis detects a spurious effect, the pipeline has residual bias.

**Diagnostics and sensitivity analysis.**
Report covariate distributions in the trial and RWD populations before and after harmonization. Report standardized mean differences for all covariates. If transportability weights are used, report the weight distribution and effective sample size. As a sensitivity analysis, vary the set of covariates used for harmonization and assess stability. If a key covariate is available in the trial but not in the RWD (or vice versa), conduct a quantitative bias analysis for the missing confounder.

## Pitfall: Reporting Without Context or Interpretation

**Mistake:**
Reporting only a point estimate (or a single relative measure) without absolute risks, assumptions, or limitations.

**Why it matters:**
Causal results are only meaningful relative to the estimand, assumptions, and the population. A risk ratio of 0.75 without context could mean a reduction from 40% to 30% (clinically important) or from 0.04% to 0.03% (clinically negligible). Without explicit statement of the estimand, the reader cannot assess what question the analysis answers. Without discussion of assumptions, the reader cannot assess the credibility of the answer. Without diagnostics, the reader cannot assess whether the analysis was well-executed.

**RWE example.** A manuscript reports that a new antidepressant "reduces suicidal ideation (OR = 0.68, 95% CI: 0.52-0.89, p = 0.005)." The reader is not told: (a) the baseline rate of suicidal ideation in the comparator group (is the absolute risk reduction 1% or 10%?), (b) the estimand (intention-to-treat analog? per-protocol?), (c) how treatment switching was handled (did patients who switched from the comparator to the new drug contribute to the comparator arm?), (d) whether positivity was adequate (were there patients in the data who would never receive the comparator?), or (e) whether any sensitivity analysis for unmeasured confounding was conducted.

**Avoid by:**
- Reporting absolute risk estimates alongside contrasts (risk differences, survival probabilities) so that the clinical significance is apparent.
- Transparently stating identification assumptions and their plausibility. At minimum: exchangeability (what confounders were adjusted for, and what might be missing), positivity (was overlap adequate?), and consistency (is the treatment version well-defined?).
- Discussing limitations, diagnostics, and sensitivity analyses.
- Framing results in terms of the hypothetical intervention and estimand, not in terms of the statistical model.
- Using the reporting checklist provided at the end of this chapter.

**Diagnostics and sensitivity analysis.**
Verify that the manuscript or report includes: (a) the estimand in plain language, (b) absolute risks in both arms, (c) the identification assumptions and their plausibility, (d) at least one diagnostic (e.g., propensity score overlap, weight distribution), and (e) at least one sensitivity analysis (e.g., E-value, negative control). If any of these are missing, the report is incomplete. As a robustness check, present results on multiple scales (risk difference, risk ratio, and number needed to treat) to assess whether the qualitative conclusion is consistent across measures.

## Pitfall: Failure to Specify the Target Trial Protocol
### Design-first failures leading to immortal time bias and related biases

**Mistake:**
Proceeding directly to statistical modeling without first specifying the hypothetical randomized experiment that the observational analysis aims to emulate.

**Why it matters:**
Most structural biases in pharmacoepidemiology; immortal time bias, selection bias from conditioning on the future, time-zero misalignment, prevalent-user bias; arise from failures at the design stage, not the estimation stage. When the target trial is not specified, analysts make implicit design decisions that often do not correspond to any coherent experiment. The result is an analysis that answers an ill-defined question, regardless of how sophisticated the estimator is.

Immortal time bias is the most common consequence. It arises when follow-up time is attributed to the treatment group during a period when the outcome could not have occurred or when the patient had not yet initiated treatment. In a well-specified target trial, this cannot happen: follow-up begins at time zero, which is the moment when eligibility is met and treatment strategy is assigned. If the observational emulation allows time zero to differ from treatment initiation; for example, by defining "treated" patients as those who eventually received the drug and backdating follow-up to cohort entry; the treated group accumulates outcome-free person-time that is not at risk, biasing the comparison in favor of treatment.

**RWE example.** A study of a new biologic for inflammatory bowel disease defines the exposed group as patients who received the biologic at any point during follow-up. Follow-up starts at diagnosis for all patients. Patients in the exposed group contribute months or years of person-time before biologic initiation during which they could not have an exposure-attributed event. This immortal time inflates the denominator of the exposed group's event rate, making the drug appear protective even if it has no effect (or is harmful). The fix is to align time zero with biologic initiation, using a new-user design with an active comparator.

**Avoid by:**
- Writing out the target trial protocol (using the checklist in the introduction) before any data analysis. The protocol forces explicit decisions about eligibility, time zero, treatment strategies, and outcome definitions.
- Ensuring that time zero is the moment when eligibility is met AND the treatment strategy begins. Patients are followed from this point forward. There is no "pre-exposure" follow-up.
- Using a **new-user (incident-user) design** to avoid prevalent-user bias (see Pitfall 15).
- Verifying that no analysis decision depends on future information (i.e., information not available at time zero).

**Diagnostics and sensitivity analysis.**
Draw a timeline for each treatment arm showing when eligibility is met, when treatment starts, and when follow-up begins. If these do not coincide, investigate whether immortal time bias is present. As a sensitivity analysis, re-estimate the effect under an alternative time zero definition (e.g., shifting time zero by 30, 60, or 90 days) and assess whether the estimate is stable. An estimate that changes dramatically with small shifts in time zero is likely contaminated by immortal time bias.

## Pitfall: Misdefinition of Time Origin and Treatment Initiation
### New-user versus prevalent-user misalignment

**Mistake:**
Including patients who have already been using the treatment of interest at study entry (prevalent users), or failing to define a clear time zero for patients in the comparator group.

**Why it matters:**
Prevalent-user bias arises because patients who are currently on treatment are a selected subgroup: they have tolerated the treatment, have not experienced early adverse events, and may have a different prognosis than patients who are just starting treatment. Including them biases the analysis toward the appearance of benefit (or, for safety studies, toward the appearance of safety) because the high-risk early period has already been survived.

For the comparator group, a parallel problem arises: if "non-users" are defined simply as patients who never receive the drug during follow-up, time zero is undefined. These patients may enter the analysis at any point; at diagnosis, at a clinic visit, or at some arbitrary calendar date; creating a heterogeneous comparator population with no clear moment of treatment assignment.

**RWE example.** A study compares patients "on metformin" (identified from prevalent prescriptions at a cross-sectional date) to patients "not on metformin." The metformin group has been tolerating the drug for a median of three years and has survived any early side effects. The comparator group includes patients who may have never been candidates for metformin, patients who tried and stopped metformin years ago, and patients who would later initiate metformin. The comparison is not between starting metformin versus not starting metformin; it is between being an established metformin user versus a heterogeneous non-user population. This does not correspond to any well-defined intervention.

**Avoid by:**
- Using a **new-user (incident-user) design.** Include only patients at the time of treatment initiation. Time zero is the date of first prescription, dispensation, or administration.
- Defining an **active comparator** that provides a clear time zero for the comparator group. For example, compare initiators of Drug A with initiators of Drug B. Both groups have a well-defined time zero (the date of initiation), and both are starting a new treatment, reducing confounding by indication.
- If a non-user comparator is necessary, define time zero using an analogous clinical event (e.g., the date of diagnosis, or the date of the clinical visit at which treatment candidacy is established) and ensure that eligibility criteria are applied at that date.

**Diagnostics and sensitivity analysis.**
Report the distribution of prior treatment duration among "users" to verify that the cohort consists of new users. If some prevalent users are inadvertently included, assess sensitivity by restricting to patients with no prior prescriptions in progressively longer lookback windows (e.g., 6 months, 12 months, 24 months). If the estimate changes substantially with the lookback window, prevalent-user bias is likely present. As an additional check, plot the hazard function over time since treatment initiation. A decreasing hazard in the first weeks or months (the "depletion of susceptibles" pattern) suggests prevalent-user contamination or early-event selection.

## Pitfall: Treatment Versioning and Ill-Defined Interventions
### Dose, formulation, switching, and combination therapy

**Mistake:**
Defining the treatment as a broad category (e.g., "SGLT2 inhibitor use") without specifying dose, formulation, route, duration, or rules for treatment modifications.

**Why it matters:**
The consistency assumption requires that the treatment variable maps to a well-defined intervention: if we say patient $i$ received treatment $A = 1$, we must be able to specify what that means in sufficient detail that the counterfactual outcome $Y(1)$ is unambiguous. When "treatment" encompasses multiple doses, formulations, or treatment trajectories, the causal estimand is a weighted average over an undefined mixture of interventions, and the estimate may not correspond to any implementable policy.

**RWE example.** A study compares "statin users" to "non-users." The statin group includes patients on low-dose atorvastatin 10 mg daily, high-dose rosuvastatin 40 mg daily, patients who started one statin and switched to another, and patients who took statins intermittently. The "effect of statin use" is an uninterpretable average over these heterogeneous treatment courses. A prescriber cannot act on the finding because it does not correspond to any specific prescribing decision.

**Avoid by:**
- Specifying the treatment strategy in the target trial protocol with sufficient precision: the drug (or drug class, if a class effect is the target), dose range, formulation, route, intended duration, and rules for switching or dose adjustment.
- When the intervention is inherently complex (e.g., a treatment algorithm with dose titration and switching rules), consider defining the strategy as a **sustained treatment strategy** (e.g., "initiate Drug A at dose X and continue for at least 6 months, with switching permitted only to Drug B if intolerance develops"). This is the treatment strategy that LTMLE and longitudinal MSMs can target.
- When dose is continuous or varies over time, consider a **modified treatment policy** (MTP) that defines the intervention as a shift from the natural treatment value rather than a fixed dose. For example, "reduce daily opioid dose by 20% relative to the dose the patient would naturally receive."
- Reporting which treatment versions are included in the analysis and assessing effect modification by dose or formulation.

**Diagnostics and sensitivity analysis.**
Tabulate the distribution of treatment versions (dose, formulation, duration) within the "treated" group. If the distribution is highly heterogeneous, assess whether the treatment effect varies across versions (e.g., estimate separately by dose category). As a sensitivity analysis, restrict the analysis to the most common treatment version and compare with the pooled estimate. If they differ, the pooled estimate is driven by treatment version composition, and the estimand should be refined.

## Pitfall: Confusing Overlap Failure With a Modeling Problem
### The estimand is not supported by the data

**Mistake:**
Responding to positivity violations by changing the model (e.g., adding regularization, truncating weights more aggressively, using a different machine learning algorithm) rather than recognizing that the target estimand may not be identifiable from the available data.

**Why it matters:**
When certain covariate strata have near-zero probability of receiving one treatment, the problem is not that the model is wrong; it is that the data do not contain the information needed to estimate the causal effect in those strata. No statistical method can create information that does not exist. Attempts to "fix" the problem by model adjustments (e.g., more aggressive weight truncation, stronger regularization of the propensity score) implicitly change the estimand without acknowledging it, because the effective target population shifts toward the region of overlap.

This pitfall is distinct from Pitfall 3, which describes the symptoms of positivity violations. The present pitfall addresses the common response of treating the problem as a modeling issue rather than an identifiability issue, and provides a decision framework for when to change the estimand rather than the estimator.

**RWE example.** A study compares surgical versus medical management of carotid stenosis. Patients over 85 with severe comorbidities are almost never offered surgery. The analyst observes extreme propensity score weights for these patients and responds by truncating weights at the 99th percentile. This truncation implicitly shifts the target population away from the elderly and severely ill patients; precisely the patients for whom the treatment comparison is most uncertain. The analyst reports the result as the ATE for the full population, but it is effectively an ATE for a restricted population defined by the truncation threshold.

### When to restrict, repair, or report

1. **Restrict the population.** If the positivity violation is concentrated in identifiable subgroups (e.g., patients over 85 with eGFR < 15), exclude them from the analysis and state the revised target population. This is honest and interpretable.

2. **Repair the estimand.** If the scientific question permits, replace the static intervention with a **stochastic intervention**, **incremental propensity score intervention**, or **modified treatment policy** that does not require extrapolation beyond the observed data. These estimands are defined by interventions that respect the natural treatment probability rather than overriding it (see Pitfall 3 for details).

3. **Report the limitation.** If neither restriction nor repair produces a scientifically meaningful alternative, state that the original estimand is not credibly identifiable and present the available evidence (e.g., estimated effects within the region of overlap) as partial information.

**Avoid by:**
- Distinguishing between estimator instability (a modeling problem) and estimand non-identifiability (a data support problem). The test is conceptual: if the data-generating process guarantees that certain patients never receive one treatment, no model can estimate the treatment effect in those patients.
- Using the structured diagnostic workflow from Pitfall 3 to characterize the nature and extent of positivity violations before choosing a response.
- Reporting any truncation, restriction, or estimand change transparently, including the characteristics of excluded patients.

**Diagnostics and sensitivity analysis.**
Report the propensity score distribution and identify the subgroups driving positivity violations. If weights are truncated, report the effective estimand (i.e., the population defined by the truncation threshold) and compare estimates at multiple truncation levels. As a sensitivity analysis, estimate the effect using a stochastic intervention with varying shift parameters (e.g., odds ratio shift of 1.5, 2.0, 3.0) and assess whether the direction and magnitude of the effect are stable across these alternative estimands.

## Pitfall: Interference and Spillovers Ignored
### Violated no-interference assumption

**Mistake:**
Assuming that one patient's treatment does not affect another patient's outcome (the stable unit treatment value assumption, or SUTVA) in settings where interference is plausible.

**Why it matters:**
The standard causal inference framework assumes no interference: the potential outcome for patient $i$ depends only on patient $i$'s treatment, not on the treatments of other patients. When this assumption is violated; for example, in vaccine studies where vaccination of one person reduces infection risk for their contacts, or in hospital settings where antibiotic prescribing for one patient affects resistance patterns for other patients; the standard ATE is not well-defined, and methods that ignore interference can be substantially biased.

In pharmacoepidemiology, interference is less commonly discussed but can arise in several contexts: household-level exposures (e.g., secondhand smoke, shared medications), institutional prescribing patterns (e.g., hospital formulary decisions that affect all patients), community-level interventions (e.g., public health campaigns), and market-level effects (e.g., when widespread adoption of a drug changes the population of remaining non-users through selection).

**RWE example.** A study of influenza antiviral treatment estimates the effect of treating an index case on household transmission. The outcome for household contacts depends on whether the index case was treated (interference). Standard person-level IPTW applied to this setting would yield a biased estimate because it does not account for the fact that one patient's treatment affects another's outcome. The estimand must be defined at the household level (e.g., the effect of treating the index case on the probability that at least one household contact becomes infected).

**Avoid by:**
- Assessing whether the no-interference assumption is scientifically plausible in the study setting. If patients share a household, institution, or prescribing physician, interference may be present.
- When interference is plausible, defining the estimand at the cluster level (e.g., household, hospital, community) and using methods designed for interference, such as cluster-randomized trial analogs or inverse probability weighting with interference-aware estimands.
- When interference is suspected but cannot be fully addressed, conducting a sensitivity analysis for the magnitude of spillover effects.

**Diagnostics and sensitivity analysis.**
If the data contain cluster information (household, physician, hospital), test whether outcomes within clusters are correlated conditional on measured confounders, beyond what individual-level models predict. As a sensitivity analysis, re-estimate the treatment effect using cluster-robust standard errors and compare with individual-level standard errors. If cluster-robust standard errors are substantially larger, unmodeled within-cluster dependence (potentially due to interference) is inflating precision in the standard analysis.

## Pitfall: Overstating External Validity Without Transportability Assessment
### Generalizing beyond the study sample

**Mistake:**
Interpreting a treatment effect estimated in one population as applicable to a different target population without formal assessment of transportability.

**Why it matters:**
Every causal estimate is specific to the population in which it is identified. When the study population differs from the target population in ways that modify the treatment effect, applying the study estimate directly to the target population will be misleading. This is a concern both for extrapolating from RCTs (which typically enroll selected, healthier populations) and for extrapolating from one RWD source to another (e.g., from a claims database to a clinical registry).

Transportability requires that (a) the effect modifiers are measured in both the study and target populations, and (b) the treatment effect conditional on those modifiers is the same in both populations. Formal transportability methods re-weight or standardize the study estimate to the target population distribution of effect modifiers. Without this step, the analyst is implicitly assuming that the study population is representative of the target; an assumption that is rarely justified and should be assessed.

**RWE example.** A randomized trial of a new antihypertensive enrolls patients aged 40-70 with uncomplicated hypertension and no major comorbidities. The trial finds a 15% relative risk reduction in cardiovascular events. A health system wants to apply this estimate to its patient population, which includes many patients over 75 with diabetes, CKD, and heart failure. The treatment effect in this population may differ due to age-related pharmacokinetic changes, drug interactions, competing risks of death, and effect modification by comorbidity burden. Directly applying the trial estimate overstates the certainty of the expected benefit.

**Avoid by:**
- Stating the target population explicitly in the estimand specification and assessing whether the study population is representative.
- When the study and target populations differ, using formal **transportability** or **generalizability** methods to re-weight the study estimate. These methods require measurement of effect modifiers in both populations and use IPTW or TMLE to standardize the estimate to the target population.
- When formal transportability is not feasible (e.g., because effect modifiers are not measured in the target population), acknowledging the limitation explicitly and discussing the direction and potential magnitude of the difference.

**Diagnostics and sensitivity analysis.**
Compare the distribution of key effect modifiers between the study population and the target population. Compute standardized differences and assess whether the populations are comparable. If transportability weights are used, report the weight distribution and effective sample size. As a sensitivity analysis, vary the set of effect modifiers used for transportability adjustment and assess stability. If the transported estimate is sensitive to which modifiers are included, external validity is uncertain.

## Pitfall: Inappropriate Handling of Competing Risks
### When another event precludes the outcome of interest

**Mistake:**
Applying standard survival analysis methods (Kaplan-Meier, Cox regression) to estimate the risk of an event in the presence of competing risks that preclude its occurrence, without acknowledging that the estimand has changed.

**Why it matters:**
Standard Kaplan-Meier and Cox regression treat competing events as censoring. This implicitly estimates the event rate in a hypothetical world where the competing event does not exist; a quantity that may not be scientifically meaningful. For example, estimating the "risk of cardiac hospitalization" using Kaplan-Meier while censoring at non-cardiac death estimates the risk that would be observed if non-cardiac death were eliminated. This quantity is higher than the actual risk observed in the real world (where some patients die before they can be hospitalized) and may not correspond to any clinically actionable question.

**RWE example.** A study of hip fracture risk in elderly patients on bisphosphonates censors patients who die from any cause. In a population with high mortality (e.g., patients over 85 in long-term care), a substantial proportion die before a hip fracture can occur. Censoring at death inflates the estimated fracture risk because it describes the risk in a hypothetical world without mortality. The clinician, however, needs to know the actual probability of fracture in the real world where patients also die; i.e., the cumulative incidence accounting for the competing risk of death.

**Avoid by:**
- Determining whether a competing event is present and, if so, defining the estimand accordingly. Use the competing risks decision rule from Pitfall 8: does the competing event change the meaning of the estimand?
- If the goal is the real-world probability of the event (accounting for competing risks), use the **cumulative incidence function** (Aalen-Johansen estimator) or cause-specific subdistribution methods.
- If the goal is the effect of treatment on the event-specific hazard (the rate among those still at risk), use cause-specific hazard models with the understanding that this conditions on survival and does not directly give the probability of the event.
- For causal inference with competing risks, consider the **separable effects** framework, TMLE-based competing risk estimators (e.g., the `concrete` package), or defining a composite endpoint that includes both the event and the competing event.

**Diagnostics and sensitivity analysis.**
Report the rate of the competing event by treatment arm. If competing events are common (e.g., >5% of the population), compare estimates from standard Kaplan-Meier (censoring at competing event) with cumulative incidence estimates (treating competing events as competing risks). If the two diverge substantially, the choice of handling method materially affects the conclusion. As a sensitivity analysis, define a composite endpoint (event + competing event) and estimate the treatment effect on the composite.

## Pitfall: Measurement Error and Outcome Misclassification
### Imperfect data do not produce perfect estimates

**Mistake:**
Assuming that treatment, covariates, and outcomes are measured without error in administrative or electronic health record data.

**Why it matters:**
Measurement error in treatment creates misclassification of the exposure, biasing the treatment effect toward the null (for non-differential misclassification) or in either direction (for differential misclassification). Measurement error in confounders produces residual confounding even when the true confounders are conceptually included in the model, because adjustment for a mismeasured confounder does not fully remove its confounding effect. Outcome misclassification can inflate or deflate event rates and bias treatment effect estimates.

In claims-based pharmacoepidemiology, all three forms of measurement error are common. Treatment exposure is typically measured by prescription dispensation, which may not reflect actual medication use (patients may not fill prescriptions, may fill but not take them, or may share medications). Confounders such as BMI, smoking status, or disease severity are often unavailable or measured with substantial error. Outcomes defined by diagnosis codes have imperfect sensitivity and specificity that varies across clinical settings and patient populations.

**RWE example.** A study of oral anticoagulant safety uses pharmacy dispensation records to define treatment exposure and ICD-10 codes to define bleeding outcomes. Patients who fill a prescription but do not take the medication are classified as exposed, diluting the apparent treatment effect. Bleeding events detected only through hospitalization codes miss outpatient bleeding events (imperfect sensitivity), while some coded bleeding events are rule-out diagnoses (imperfect specificity). Together, these measurement errors can attenuate or distort the estimated treatment effect.

**Avoid by:**
- Validating treatment, covariate, and outcome definitions against chart review, linked clinical data, or published validation studies. Report the positive predictive value (PPV) and sensitivity of outcome definitions when available.
- Conducting **quantitative bias analysis for misclassification**: specify the sensitivity and specificity of the outcome definition (or exposure definition) and compute the bias-adjusted estimate under a range of plausible misclassification parameters.
- When confounder measurement error is suspected, using validation sub-studies or external calibration data to assess the magnitude of residual confounding due to mismeasurement.
- Considering the use of **probabilistic bias analysis** that jointly models misclassification of exposure, outcome, and confounders.

**Diagnostics and sensitivity analysis.**
Report the validation characteristics (PPV, sensitivity, specificity) of key variable definitions. If validation data are not available, cite published estimates from the same or similar data sources. Conduct a quantitative bias analysis assuming plausible ranges of sensitivity and specificity for the outcome definition (e.g., sensitivity 70-90%, specificity 95-99%) and report how the estimated treatment effect changes. If the conclusion is sensitive to misclassification parameters within the plausible range, this should be acknowledged as a limitation.

## Reporting Checklist for Target Trial Emulations

Transparent reporting is essential for the credibility of causal analyses using observational data. The following checklist, aligned with emerging best practices for target trial emulation studies, should be used alongside the RECORD and STROBE guidelines.

::: {.callout-note}
## Reporting checklist

**Design**

1. Explicitly label the study as a target trial emulation.
2. Present the target trial protocol (eligibility, time zero, treatment strategies, outcome, causal contrast, analysis plan) as a structured table.
3. Present the observational emulation mapping: how each protocol element was implemented in the data source.

**Estimand**

4. State the estimand using the estimand specification template (population, treatment, outcome, intercurrent event handling, summary measure).
5. Distinguish the estimand from the statistical model. The estimand is the target; the model is the tool.

**Identification and assumptions**

6. State the identification assumptions (exchangeability, positivity, consistency) and discuss their plausibility in the study context.
7. Describe the confounder adjustment strategy and justify the covariate set with reference to a DAG or subject-matter knowledge.

**Estimation**

8. Describe the estimator (g-computation, IPTW, TMLE, LTMLE, etc.) and link it to the estimand it targets.
9. Describe nuisance model fitting (e.g., SuperLearner library, cross-fitting) and report cross-validated risk.

**Diagnostics**

10. Report propensity score overlap and weight distributions (for IPTW/TMLE).
11. Report covariate balance after weighting (SMDs).
12. Report influence curve diagnostics and any observations with extreme influence (for TMLE).
13. Report calibration of nuisance models.

**Sensitivity analyses**

14. Report at least one sensitivity analysis for unmeasured confounding (E-value, quantitative bias analysis, or negative control).
15. Report design sensitivity analyses (varying time zero, grace period, covariate set) as appropriate.
16. State clearly how conclusions change (or do not change) under each sensitivity analysis.

**Results**

17. Report absolute risks in each treatment arm alongside contrasts.
18. Report results on at least one absolute scale (risk difference, survival probability, RMST) in addition to any relative measures.
:::

## Summary

Common pitfalls arise from misalignment between analytic choices and scientific questions, misspecification, unchecked assumptions, and overinterpretation. Avoiding them requires:

- Explicit use of the Causal Roadmap
- Diagnostics aligned with identification risks (for example, overlap and censoring)
- Sensitivity analyses for plausible violations
- Careful communication of estimands and assumptions

These practices improve credibility, reproducibility, and decision relevance.

The expanded set of pitfalls in this chapter can be distilled into the following ten principles:

::: {.callout-important}
## Ten principles for credible causal inference in RWE

1. **Specify the target trial first.** Write the target trial protocol; eligibility, time zero, treatment strategies, outcome, causal contrast; before touching the data. Most structural biases (immortal time, prevalent user, time-zero misalignment) are prevented by this step.

2. **Define the estimand before choosing the estimator.** The estimand determines the identification conditions and the statistical functional. The estimator is chosen to estimate that functional. Reversing this order leads to analyses that answer unintended questions.

3. **Respect the temporal structure.** Adjust only for pre-treatment covariates in the propensity score and baseline outcome model. If time-varying confounders are affected by prior treatment, use longitudinal methods (LTMLE, longitudinal MSMs). Never condition on post-treatment variables unless the estimand explicitly requires it.

4. **Diagnose positivity before estimating.** Assess overlap through propensity score distributions, weight diagnostics, and effective sample sizes. When positivity fails, change the target population or the estimand; do not force an answer by truncating weights and pretending the estimand is unchanged.

5. **Use doubly robust estimation with flexible nuisance models and cross-fitting.** TMLE and AIPW provide protection against misspecification of a single nuisance function. SuperLearner reduces dependence on parametric assumptions. Cross-fitting (CV-TMLE) guards against overfitting when the learner library includes highly adaptive algorithms.

6. **Choose estimands that match the decision context.** Avoid defaulting to hazard ratios or odds ratios. Prefer risk differences, survival probabilities, cumulative incidence, or RMST; measures that are collapsible, causally interpretable, and clinically actionable. Handle competing risks explicitly.

7. **Treat sensitivity analysis as mandatory, not optional.** Every observational analysis should include at least one assessment of robustness to unmeasured confounding (E-value, quantitative bias analysis, or negative control) and at least one design sensitivity analysis.

8. **Report transparently.** State the estimand, the identification assumptions, the diagnostics, and the sensitivity analyses. Report absolute risks. Do not hide important limitations in supplementary materials.

9. **Distinguish identifiability from estimation.** When the data cannot support the estimand (structural positivity violations, unmeasured confounders known to be important), no estimator can fix the problem. Acknowledge the limitation, restrict the population, or revise the estimand.

10. **Iterate.** The Causal Roadmap is not linear. Diagnostics may reveal that the original estimand is not supported, requiring a return to the design stage. Sensitivity analyses may suggest that a different covariate set is needed. Treat this iteration as a feature of rigorous analysis, not a failure of planning.
:::
